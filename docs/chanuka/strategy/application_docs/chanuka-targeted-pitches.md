## PART ONE: FELLOWSHIPS

### 1. Shuttleworth Fellowship
**What it funds:** Radical openness. Individuals building open-source tools that shift systemic power. €100K+ over one year, renewable. One of the most founder-aligned fellowships in existence.

**Why this is the right fit:** Chanuka is an open-source parliamentary accountability engine. The Shuttleworth model is explicitly for builders who have already started and need runway to go further — not idea-stage applicants. The codebase is operational. The pilot is scoped. This is exactly the profile they fund.

**Pitch:**

Democratic accountability in Kenya fails not because citizens are apathetic but because the infrastructure to make accountability consequential was never built. Parliamentary data exists. It is technically public. It is practically inaccessible — buried in formats, language, and systems that serve institutional convenience, not citizen use.

Chanuka is the open-source engine that converts that inaccessible data into electoral consequence. It ingests parliamentary records, translates legislation into plain language, detects constitutional violations in bills, and generates constituency-level accountability scorecards that measure the gap between how MPs vote and what their constituents want. The codebase is operational. The Nairobi pilot — 17 constituencies, 4.3 million constituents — is the first demonstration that the accountability loop functions with real stakes.

The Shuttleworth Fellowship would fund the completion of that loop: the constitutional violation detection engine, the trojan bill identifier, the USSD layer that extends accountability beyond smartphone users to every Kenyan with a basic handset. These are not features. They are the components that convert a transparency tool into genuine power redistribution.

The case for openness is built into the architecture. Chanuka is designed to be forked — adapted by civic technologists in Uganda, Ghana, Nigeria, and any parliamentary democracy where the same data-inaccessibility problem exists. The Nairobi pilot is the proof of concept. Open-source publication is the multiplication strategy.

**What to emphasise in the application:** The open-source architecture, the replicability model, the specific technical gaps the fellowship would close, and your theory of why parliamentary data is a power resource that currently only serves the powerful.

### 2. Obama Foundation Leaders: Africa
**What it funds:** Emerging African leaders with demonstrated community impact and a vision for systemic change. Cohort-based. Heavy emphasis on values, narrative, and the person behind the work.

**Why this is the right fit:** The Obama Foundation is not funding platforms. It is funding people whose character, vision, and demonstrated commitment make them worth investing in. The pitch here is about you as a leader, with Chanuka as evidence of what that leadership looks like in practice.

**Pitch:**

I grew up understanding that the distance between a government and its citizens is not natural — it is manufactured. Complexity is a governance strategy. Opacity is a power structure. The citizen who cannot read the bill that will raise their taxes is not uninformed by accident. They are uninformed by design.

Chanuka is my answer to that design. I built Kenya's first parliamentary accountability engine — independently, without institutional backing — because I believed the infrastructure for democratic consequence needed to exist regardless of whether someone funded it first. The platform tracks MP voting records by constituency, translates legislation into plain language, and generates accountability scorecards that measure the gap between what representatives promise and what they do. It is operational. The Nairobi pilot is ready.

But the platform is not the point. The point is what it demonstrates: that ordinary citizens, given the right tools, can hold extraordinary power accountable between elections and not just during them. Kenya's Gen Z movement proved that civic energy exists in abundance. What it lacked — what every leaderless movement eventually runs into — is institutional knowledge. The ability to distinguish which battle is worth fighting, where legislative leverage exists, and how to measure whether you are winning. Chanuka is that knowledge, made accessible, in a format that compounds with use.

The Obama Foundation's investment in African leaders is premised on a belief I share: that the solutions to Africa's governance failures will come from Africans who understand the terrain, not from institutions that arrive with templates. I am building from inside the problem. I am asking for the fellowship that helps me build further and faster.

**What to emphasise in the application:** Personal narrative and the values that drove independent development before external validation. The Gen Z movement as the constituency. The leader as multiplier, not just builder.

### 3. Mozilla Fellowship — Civic Tech / Internet Health Track
**What it funds:** Technologists working at the intersection of open internet, civic power, and accountability. Typically embedded with a host organisation. Stipend plus project funding.

**Why this is the right fit:** Mozilla's civic tech track is explicitly interested in how technology mediates the relationship between citizens and institutions. Chanuka sits precisely at that intersection: it uses web scraping, NLP-based plain language translation, and data visualisation to redistribute access to parliamentary information.

**Pitch:**

The internet promised to democratise access to information. In civic life, that promise has largely failed — not because the information does not exist online, but because it exists in formats, language, and architectures that serve institutional record-keeping rather than citizen use. Kenya's parliamentary data is online. It is practically inaccessible. The gap between technical availability and functional accessibility is where democratic accountability dies.

Chanuka is an open-source civic technology platform that closes that gap. It ingests parliamentary records through an automated scraping pipeline, processes legislation through a plain-language translation engine, and generates constituency-level MP accountability scorecards. The technical stack is built on the principle that civic data is a public resource that should function as one — not locked in PDFs, obscured by procedural language, or navigable only to those with insider knowledge.

The Mozilla Fellowship would support two specific technical developments with outsized civic impact. First, the constitutional violation detection layer — an NLP model trained to flag when bills contain provisions that conflict with Kenya's constitution, surfacing those flags for citizens in plain language before legislation passes. Second, the USSD access layer — a complete feature-phone interface that makes the accountability platform functional for Kenyans without smartphones, addressing the digital divide that most civic tech platforms ignore by design.

These are not incremental features. They represent the difference between a platform that serves the digitally privileged and one that serves the democratic mandate. Mozilla's investment in internet health should extend to the civic infrastructure that makes democratic participation possible — not just the infrastructure that makes commercial participation convenient.

**What to emphasise in the application:** Technical specifics of the NLP pipeline, the open-source architecture, the USSD layer as an internet health argument, and the replicability across other parliamentary democracies.

### 4. Ashoka Fellowship
**What it funds:** Social entrepreneurs with a systems-change idea that is already being demonstrated, and the capacity to spread it. Lifetime fellowship. Primarily recognition, network, and seed support.

**Why this is the right fit:** Ashoka funds the pattern-change, not just the project. The pitch here is that Chanuka is a replicable model for converting parliamentary opacity into accountability — applicable across Sub-Saharan Africa and beyond. You are not just building a Kenyan platform. You are demonstrating a model.

**Pitch:**

Every parliamentary democracy in Sub-Saharan Africa shares a structural failure: the data that would allow citizens to hold their representatives accountable exists, is technically public, and is functionally inaccessible. The problem is not unique to Kenya. The solution does not need to be built from scratch in each country.

Chanuka is both a platform and a proof of concept for a replicable model. In Kenya, it converts parliamentary records into constituency-level accountability scores — measuring the gap between how MPs vote and what their constituents want, in a format citizens, journalists, and community organisers can actually use. The Nairobi pilot covers 17 constituencies and 4.3 million constituents. The scraping infrastructure, the plain-language translation engine, and the accountability scoring methodology are all designed to be adapted by civic technologists in any parliamentary context.

The systems change Ashoka funds is not the platform. It is the shift in the relationship between citizens and legislative power that the platform enables. When accountability becomes visible, specific, and attached to names and voting records, the calculus for every MP changes. The cost of voting against constituents' interests rises. That rise — multiplied across hundreds of constituencies, sustained across election cycles — is what democratic accountability actually looks like. Chanuka is the infrastructure for that shift. The fellowship is the support to demonstrate it at scale.

**What to emphasise in the application:** The replicability model, the systemic theory of change, and the specific mechanism by which Chanuka changes incentive structures for elected officials — not just informs citizens.

### 5. National Endowment for Democracy (NED) — Africa Grants
**What it funds:** Democratic governance infrastructure in emerging democracies. Direct grants to organisations and individuals working on transparency, accountability, civil society, and legislative access. Active in Kenya.

**Why this is the right fit:** NED is the most directly aligned funder for Chanuka's specific function. They fund exactly this: the gap between formal democratic structure and functional democratic accountability. The pitch should be precise and operational, not visionary.

**Pitch:**

Kenya's 13th Parliament operates with formal transparency — bills are published, votes are recorded, Hansards are produced. What does not exist is the infrastructure that converts that formal transparency into functional accountability: a system that allows a citizen in Mathare to know how their MP voted on the Finance Bill, understand what that vote means for their household, and carry that knowledge into the next election cycle.

Chanuka is that infrastructure. The platform ingests parliamentary data through an operational scraping pipeline, translates legislation into plain language accessible to citizens without legal or technical training, and generates MP accountability scorecards by constituency — measuring the gap between voting behaviour and constituent sentiment. Core infrastructure is operational. The requested grant funds the Nairobi pilot: 17 constituencies, 4.3 million constituents, accountability scorecards for each MP, and distribution through civil society networks before the 2027 election cycle.

NED's mandate is not to produce information about democracy. It is to strengthen the mechanisms by which democratic systems produce accountability. Chanuka is the accountability mechanism. The existing tools — Mzalendo and equivalent transparency portals — address the information gap admirably. They do not close the accountability gap. Information tells citizens what happened. Chanuka converts what happened into electoral consequence. That distinction is the difference between transparency and accountability, and it is the gap this grant is designed to close.

**Budget ask:** Pilot completion, USSD layer development, civil society distribution partnerships, and a full-cycle accountability report for the 2027 election.

### 6. Luminate Group (Omidyar Network Civic Tech Arm)
**What it funds:** Civic tech, government transparency, and the redistribution of information power from institutions to citizens. One of the most active funders of parliamentary monitoring technology globally.

**Why this is the right fit:** Luminate explicitly funds the intersection of technology and civic power redistribution. They funded mySociety (TheyWorkForYou, UK), MySociety's African partners, and multiple parliamentary monitoring tools globally. Chanuka fits squarely in their portfolio thesis.

**Pitch:**

Luminate's thesis — that technology can shift information power from institutions to citizens — has been demonstrated in legislative monitoring contexts across the UK, Australia, and parts of Latin America. Sub-Saharan Africa remains systematically underserved, not because the problem is different but because the investment has not matched the scale of the accountability gap.

Chanuka is the Kenyan instance of that thesis, built from inside the context rather than adapted from outside it. The platform converts parliamentary opacity into constituency-level accountability data: MP voting records tracked against constituent sentiment, legislation translated into plain language, constitutional violations flagged before bills pass. The data pipeline is operational. The Nairobi pilot is the first real-world test of whether the accountability loop produces behavioural change — in citizens' engagement patterns and, over time, in MPs' voting behaviour.

The Luminate portfolio includes the tools that built this model elsewhere. Chanuka is the investment that extends it to Kenya's 47 million citizens — with an architecture designed for replication across East Africa's parliamentary democracies. The USSD layer, in particular, addresses a gap that no existing parliamentary monitoring tool on the continent has closed: access for citizens without smartphones, which in Kenya's context means reaching the constituencies where accountability deficits are most acute and electoral consequences most meaningful.

**What to emphasise:** Fit with existing Luminate portfolio, the replication model for East Africa, and the specific technical gap (USSD) that differentiates Chanuka from existing tools they may already fund.

### 7. Ford Foundation — Democracy and Civic Power
**What it funds:** Structural inequality, power redistribution, and the civic infrastructure that enables marginalised communities to participate in decisions that affect them. Multi-year grants, relationships over transactions.

**Why this is the right fit:** Ford's democracy programme is focused on the structural conditions that enable or prevent civic participation — not just access to information but access to power. The USSD layer and the constituency-level targeting make Chanuka a genuine equity argument, not just a tech argument.

**Pitch:**

The civic participation gap in Kenya is not a motivation gap. Citizens in Kibra, Mathare, and Korogocho have demonstrated, repeatedly and at personal cost, that they are willing to engage with the systems that govern their lives. What they lack is not will but infrastructure — the tools that convert civic energy into legislative consequence.

Chanuka addresses the structural dimension of that gap. The platform tracks MP voting records by constituency, translates legislation into language accessible to citizens without legal training, and generates accountability scores that measure the specific distance between representative behaviour and constituent interest. Critically, its USSD layer ensures that access is not conditioned on smartphone ownership — reaching the constituencies where accountability deficits are deepest and where formal civic engagement has historically been most exclusionary.

Ford's investment in civic power is premised on the recognition that democracy's failures are structural before they are individual. Chanuka is structural infrastructure. It does not ask citizens to work harder to access a broken system. It rebuilds the system's feedback mechanism — the accountability loop that should make representative behaviour consequential but currently does not function. The 2027 election cycle is the first real test of whether that loop, properly built and distributed, changes outcomes. This grant funds that test.

### 8. Knight Foundation — Informed and Engaged Communities
**What it funds:** The information infrastructure that enables communities to participate in civic life. Journalism, civic media, and increasingly civic technology that bridges the gap between public information and public understanding.

**Why this is the right fit:** Knight's framing of "informed communities" maps directly onto Chanuka's plain-language translation engine and its accountability scorecard — tools that convert raw parliamentary data into information citizens can actually use. The journalism angle is also genuine: Chanuka's data is a primary source for accountability reporting.

**Pitch:**

The accountability journalism that democratic systems require depends on primary source data that is currently inaccessible to most Kenyan journalists and entirely inaccessible to citizens. Parliamentary voting records exist in formats that require significant technical and legal expertise to interpret. The result is accountability reporting that is episodic, personality-driven, and unable to systematically track the gap between what representatives promise and what they do.

Chanuka converts that primary source data into structured, accessible, and shareable information — MP accountability scorecards by constituency, plain-language bill summaries, constitutional violation flags, and visualised voting patterns over time. This is not a replacement for journalism. It is the data infrastructure that makes sustained, evidence-based accountability journalism possible — and that puts that same information directly in citizens' hands so that accountability does not depend on whether a journalist picks up the story.

Knight's investment in informed communities should extend to the civic data layer that makes both journalism and direct citizen engagement possible. The Nairobi pilot generates the first systematic, constituency-level dataset of MP voting behaviour and constituent sentiment alignment in Kenya's history. That dataset is a public resource. Its creation is the investment. Its use — by journalists, by civil society, by citizens on WhatsApp channels deciding how to vote in 2027 — is the return.
