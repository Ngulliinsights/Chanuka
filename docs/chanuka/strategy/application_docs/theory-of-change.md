# Chanuka: Theory of Change
## A Granular Causal Chain from Individual Behaviour to Structural Consequence

---

> *"A theory of change that cannot name the specific person who changes their behaviour, under what conditions, because of what specific intervention, is not a theory of change. It is a theory of hope."*

---

## THE PROBLEM WITH MOST CIVIC TECH THEORIES OF CHANGE

Most parliamentary accountability platforms operate on an implicit theory that looks like this:

**Data exists → Citizens access data → Citizens are informed → Democracy improves**

Every arrow in that chain is an assumption dressed as a mechanism. *Why* do citizens access data they did not previously seek? *What specifically* changes between informed and acting? *How* does individual action aggregate into systemic democratic improvement?

The answer to each of these questions is: we do not know, we assumed, and we hoped.

Chanuka's theory of change is built differently. It starts not with the platform but with a specific person, a specific decision moment, and a specific psychological state — and traces the causal chain forward from there with the same precision applied to the structural diagnosis.

---

## LEVEL 1: THE INDIVIDUAL — WHO CHANGES, AND WHY

### The Three User Archetypes That Drive Adoption

Not all citizens change behaviour for the same reason. Chanuka's adoption model is built around three distinct archetypes, each with a different psychological entry point and a different propagation behaviour.

---

**Archetype A: The Aggrieved Constituent**

*Who:* The person who already knows, intuitively, that something is wrong. Their matatu fare went up. Their school fees increased. Their market stall was threatened with demolition. They have a grievance and a name — their MP — but no verified connection between the two.

*The psychological state:* Frustrated but unequipped. They have the motivation to act but not the specific, verifiable evidence that converts personal grievance into legitimate public accusation.

*The specific intervention:* Chanuka's accountability scorecard gives this person what they are missing: not an argument but a document. Not an accusation but a record. "MP X voted for the Finance Bill provision that raised your fuel levy. Here is the vote. Here is the date. Here is the provision number. Here is the comparison with what they promised in 2022."

*Why this produces behaviour change:* The psychological barrier for this archetype is not motivation — it is legitimacy. They feel unable to publicly accuse their MP without verified evidence. The scorecard removes that barrier. It converts private grievance into public accountability. The behaviour change is specific: this person shares the scorecard. In a WhatsApp group. At a market committee meeting. In a conversation with their church elder. The propagation is horizontal and personal.

*Adoption trigger:* A bill or policy that directly affects their daily economic life. The fuel levy. The school fee cap removal. The market licensing increase. These are the moments when the Aggrieved Constituent is searching for the connection between what happened to them and who voted for it.

---

**Archetype B: The Civic Multiplier**

*Who:* The community organiser, the church civic programme leader, the market association secretary, the university student union official, the local journalist. This person already performs accountability functions informally — they attend barazas, follow political news, relay information through community networks.

*The psychological state:* Under-resourced. They are performing a function that requires institutional infrastructure — verified data, comparative analysis, historical records — without institutional support. They are doing their best with WhatsApp forwards and newspaper clips.

*The specific intervention:* Chanuka gives this person the primary source material they have been improvising without. Not better information about what politicians are saying — verified records of what they did. The Civic Multiplier becomes significantly more effective with this resource because their existing network is already primed to receive accountability information. They are the distribution channel. Chanuka is the content that makes the channel valuable.

*Why this produces behaviour change:* The Civic Multiplier's barrier is credibility — the ability to make specific, defensible accountability claims in their community context. The scorecard gives them that credibility. It upgrades their function from community commentator to community accountability anchor. The behaviour change is amplified: one Civic Multiplier with Chanuka data reaches their entire existing network with verified information that previously did not exist.

*Adoption trigger:* A local issue — a specific constituency-level policy failure, a specific MP position that contradicts local interest. The Civic Multiplier is triggered by local salience, not national narrative. This is why constituency-level disaggregation is not a feature choice. It is the adoption mechanism.

---

**Archetype C: The Persuadable Voter**

*Who:* The person who votes — reliably — but not primarily on legislative record. They vote on personality, on ethnicity, on who showed up at their parent's funeral, on who gave the biggest harambee. They are not disengaged. They are engaged on the wrong basis, because the right basis — comparative legislative performance — has never been accessible to them in usable form.

*The psychological state:* Not ideologically committed to their MP. Open to a better argument. But the better argument has never been presented in a form they could act on. Campaign promises are not verifiable. Newspaper coverage is episodic and personality-driven. The community elder who endorses the incumbent is a more immediately credible source than a newspaper they do not read.

*The specific intervention:* The Persuadable Voter does not seek out Chanuka. Chanuka reaches them through Archetype A and Archetype B — through the WhatsApp share, the baraza presentation, the church civic session. The intervention is not pull but push: the accountability information arrives in their existing social context, endorsed by a trusted community member, attached to a specific decision they are already facing.

*Why this produces behaviour change:* This archetype changes not because they went looking for data but because the data arrived in a context that made it socially legitimate to act on it. The peer endorsement of the Civic Multiplier converts abstract accountability data into a community norm: *this is information that serious people in our community use to make decisions.* The behaviour change is the most contested and most important: this is the vote that changes.

*Adoption trigger:* Social proof from Archetype B, delivered close to the electoral decision moment. The timing matters as much as the content.

## LEVEL 2: THE PROPAGATION — HOW INDIVIDUAL CHANGE AGGREGATES

### The Diffusion Chain

Individual behaviour change is necessary but insufficient. The theory of change requires a specific account of how individual adoption aggregates into the constituency-level pressure that changes an MP's calculus.

**Step 1: Seeding through Archetype A**
The Aggrieved Constituent discovers the scorecard through a triggered search — a bill passed, a policy announced, an economic harm experienced. They share it. Target: 500 organic seeding events in Nairobi's 17 constituencies in the first six months. This is achievable without any paid distribution.

**Step 2: Amplification through Archetype B**
The Civic Multiplier receives the scorecard through their network, validates it against their existing political knowledge, and incorporates it into their existing communication channels. Each Civic Multiplier reaches an average community network of 150–400 people. Target: 80 active Civic Multipliers across the pilot constituencies. This produces estimated first-degree reach of 60,000–100,000 people without any institutional distribution campaign.

**Step 3: Normalisation as Reference Tool**
The metric that distinguishes a platform people consult from a platform people visit once is the return visit rate. The target is not that people read the scorecard once before an election. It is that the scorecard becomes the reference tool a community uses when their MP makes a public claim. "MP X says he supported the school fee cap. Let's check." This normalisation is the inflection point — when checking the record becomes a social behaviour rather than an individual one.

**Step 4: MP Awareness of Being Watched**
This is the mechanism most theories of change skip entirely. The MP's behaviour does not change because citizens have better information. It changes because the MP *knows* citizens have better information and are using it. The communication of this awareness — through constituency-level media coverage of accountability scores, through questions raised at public forums using scorecard data, through civil society organisations presenting the data to MPs directly — is a specific programmatic activity, not a side effect of platform existence. Chanuka's civil society distribution strategy is designed explicitly to make MPs aware that the record is being kept and that it is being shared.

**Step 5: Electoral Consequence Integration**
The theory of change closes at the ballot. The mechanism: accountability scorecards published as constituency-level voter education materials in the six months before the 2027 campaign period opens. Format: one page per MP, shareable on WhatsApp, readable in under two minutes, containing the voting record, the accountability score, the three most consequential votes, and the gap from their 2022 manifesto. This material is the bridge between the accountability infrastructure and the electoral moment where it produces structural consequence.

## LEVEL 3: THE MP'S CALCULUS — WHAT ACTUALLY CHANGES

### The Incentive Shift

Democratic accountability theory assumes that electoral consequence changes representative behaviour. This is correct but incomplete. The mechanism requires specification:

**Current equilibrium:** An MP in Kenya's current accountability environment can vote against constituency interests with low electoral cost because:
- The vote is not translated into plain language for constituency consumption
- The vote is not aggregated into a comparative score
- The vote is not connected to their manifesto promises
- The vote is not available to ordinary citizens in shareable form
- The vote will not appear in any campaign material in 2027

Each of these conditions currently protects unaccountable voting behaviour. Chanuka removes all five simultaneously.

**The shifted equilibrium:** An MP who knows that:
- Every vote is being tracked and scored
- The score is being shared in their constituency's WhatsApp networks
- Civil society organisations in their constituency are using the data
- The local newspaper has cited the data in coverage
- The pre-election voter education material will contain their specific record

...faces a different incentive structure. The cost of voting against constituency interest has risen. Not eliminated — there are still patronage networks, ethnic loyalties, and executive pressure that operate alongside accountability pressure. But the cost has risen. And rising costs change marginal behaviour.

**The specific behavioural predictions:**
- MPs in constituencies with high Civic Multiplier density will show earlier and more pronounced changes in publicly stated positions on contested legislation
- MPs facing the widest gap between accountability score and constituency preference will either show voting behaviour adjustment or will increase patronage distribution — both of which are detectable and trackable
- MPs in safe seats with low Civic Multiplier density will show the least behaviour change — this is a testable prediction, not a hope

## LEVEL 4: THE ADOPTION BARRIERS — AND HOW EACH IS ADDRESSED

A complete theory of change names the barriers to adoption specifically and addresses each one.

**Barrier 1: Trust in the data**
*The concern:* Citizens, particularly the Persuadable Voter, have been manipulated by partisan information before. Why should they trust Chanuka's scores?

*The mechanism:* Every data point is sourced to the parliamentary record with a direct citation. The plain-language translation is accompanied by the original legislative text. The scoring methodology is published and auditable. The constitutional review layer is conducted in partnership with named legal civil society organisations. Trust is built through transparency of method, not assertion of credibility.

**Barrier 2: Digital access asymmetry**
*The concern:* The Persuadable Voter in the constituency where accountability deficits are highest is also the voter least likely to have a smartphone and a data bundle.

*The mechanism:* The USSD layer is not an accessibility feature. It is the primary access mechanism for Archetypes A and C in below-median-smartphone constituencies. The WhatsApp distribution strategy — shareable one-page constituency reports designed for feature-phone forwarding — is a specific design decision, not an afterthought. The Civic Multiplier's role is specifically to bridge the digital access gap: one person with a smartphone translates the accountability data into community-accessible formats.

**Barrier 3: Motivation to engage**
*The concern:* Citizens are disengaged because previous engagement produced no consequence. Why will this time be different?

*The mechanism:* The return visit rate is the leading indicator for this barrier. If citizens check the scorecard once and never return, the platform has not changed the engagement pattern. The design principle that addresses this is salience triggers: the platform sends alerts to registered users when their MP votes on legislation that the platform's data suggests is relevant to their economic interest. Engagement is not requested in the abstract — it is prompted at the moment of maximum relevance.

**Barrier 4: Co-optation of the platform itself**
*The concern:* The government pressures Chanuka to remove data, change scores, or shut down.

*The mechanism:* Open-source architecture means the methodology and data exist independently of the Chanuka organisation. If the platform is shut down, the data has been published on openAFRICA and is independently accessible. The scoring methodology is a published specification that any organisation can implement. The accountability record cannot be erased because it exists in multiple independent locations. Co-optation of the organisation does not capture the infrastructure.

**Barrier 5: The 2027 timing risk**
*The concern:* If the platform is not adopted at sufficient scale before the 2027 campaign period opens, the electoral integration — the theory of change's closing mechanism — fails.

*The mechanism:* The pilot is designed around a specific adoption timeline: six months to seed through Archetype A, six months for Civic Multiplier normalisation, six months for pre-election voter education distribution. The pilot begins 18 months before the campaign period opens. This is not generous — it is the minimum required for the normalisation step. Any delay in the pilot start compresses the adoption timeline at the most consequential phase.

## LEVEL 5: THE MEASUREMENT ARCHITECTURE

A theory of change without measurement is a narrative. The causal chain must be falsifiable.

**Leading Indicators (months 1–6):**
- Organic seeding events: scorecard shares tracked through referral links
- Archetype B adoption: number of Civic Multiplier organisations incorporating Chanuka data into their existing programmes
- Search trigger events: platform traffic spikes correlated with specific legislative events

**Process Indicators (months 6–12):**
- Return visit rate: primary indicator of normalisation vs. one-time curiosity
- USSD usage in below-median-smartphone constituencies: indicator of whether the access mechanism is reaching Archetype C
- Media citations: indicator of whether the data is entering the public accountability discourse

**Outcome Indicators (months 12–18):**
- Constituent awareness of MP voting record: survey of a sample of registered voters in pilot constituencies, before and after exposure to Chanuka data
- MP public position shifts: tracking of MPs with high accountability gap scores for adjustments in stated positions on contested legislation
- Civil society active use: number of organisations using the data in advocacy and voter education programmes

**Impact Indicator (post-2027):**
- Electoral outcome correlation: the relationship between constituency-level accountability score gap and electoral outcome for the relevant MPs. This is the closing test of the theory of change. If MPs with wide accountability gaps face measurably stronger electoral challenges in constituencies with high Chanuka adoption, the causal chain is demonstrated. If they do not, the theory is wrong and must be revised.

The willingness to specify the condition under which the theory is wrong is what distinguishes a theory of change from a mission statement.

## THE SUMMARY CAUSAL CHAIN

```
SPECIFIC LEGISLATIVE EVENT
        ↓
triggers AGGRIEVED CONSTITUENT to seek verified connection
        ↓
CHANUKA SCORECARD provides verified, shareable record
        ↓
CONSTITUENT SHARES through existing social network
        ↓
CIVIC MULTIPLIER receives, validates, amplifies through community channels
        ↓
PERSUADABLE VOTER receives in trusted social context
        ↓
ACCOUNTABILITY DATA normalises as community reference tool
        ↓
MP AWARENESS of being watched rises through media, barazas, civil society use
        ↓
PRE-ELECTION VOTER EDUCATION distributes constituency accountability profiles
        ↓
PERSUADABLE VOTER approaches 2027 ballot with verified legislative record
        ↓
ELECTORAL COST of unaccountable voting rises
        ↓
MP INCENTIVE STRUCTURE shifts — on the margin, in the next parliamentary term
        ↓
ACCOUNTABILITY CULTURE normalises as expectation, not exception
```

Every arrow is a mechanism, not an assumption. Every mechanism has a named intervention, a target population, a measurement indicator, and a falsifiable prediction.

This is the causal chain. It is not complete — no theory of change in a complex political system is. But it is honest about what it claims, specific about how it works, and falsifiable about whether it succeeds.
