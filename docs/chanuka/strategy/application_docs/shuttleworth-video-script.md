# Shuttleworth Fellowship — Video Script
## 3 Minutes Exactly. Every Word Earns Its Place.

---

### PRE-RECORDING NOTES

**Format:** Straight to camera. No slides, no screen share, no B-roll. Just you.
**Setting:** Clean background — a wall with one meaningful object, not a blank wall (which reads as staged) and not a cluttered room (which reads as careless). Natural light from the side, not behind you.
**Pace:** Slower than feels natural. You know this material. They do not. Every sentence needs landing time.
**Tone:** Not a pitch. A conversation with someone intelligent who has thirty seconds of patience before they decide whether you are worth the next three minutes.

Record minimum five takes. Watch them back without sound first — your physical presence and conviction read before your words do. Then watch with sound. The take you use will not be the first one.

---

## THE SCRIPT

---

### OPENING — 0:00 to 0:30
*[Look directly at camera. Pause one beat before speaking. Do not smile — that is a performance. Be present.]*

"Last year, Kenya's parliament passed a Finance Bill that raised the cost of living for millions of people.

The protests that followed were extraordinary — millions in the streets, young people who understood exactly what was being done to them.

But here is what they did not have: the name of the MP who voted for the bill in their constituency. The voting record. The gap between what that MP promised and what they did.

That information existed. It was technically public. It was practically inaccessible.

And so the protest was powerful — and it produced no lasting electoral consequence."

*[Pause. Let that land.]*

---

### THE ARGUMENT — 0:30 to 1:15
*[Shift slightly — this is where you become precise, not emotional.]*

"I have spent a long time studying why democratic systems fail. Not at the level of philosophy — people still want self-determination. At the level of infrastructure.

The accountability loop was never completed. Citizens were given elections and parliaments. They were not given the mechanism that makes elections and parliaments consequential: the ability to measure what their representatives do, hold them to it, and make the gap between promise and performance an electoral fact.

Transparency tools have been the wrong investment. We have built platforms that tell citizens what happened. We have not built the mechanism that makes what happened matter.

That is the distinction Chanuka is built on. Not transparency. Consequence."

---

### THE PLATFORM — 1:15 to 1:55
*[Concrete now. Specific. This is where you demonstrate that you are a builder, not a theorist.]*

"Chanuka is an open-source parliamentary accountability engine. It is operational.

It ingests Kenya's National Assembly data through an automated scraping pipeline. It translates legislation into plain language — not summaries, not editorials, the actual bill in language a citizen without legal training can read and act on. It generates constituency-level MP accountability scorecards: a quantified measure of the gap between how your representative voted and what your community wants.

The Nairobi pilot covers seventeen constituencies and 4.3 million constituents.

Three components are in active development: a constitutional violation detection layer that flags when bills conflict with Kenya's constitution before they pass — the 'trojan bill' problem. An electoral consequence layer that ties voting records to the 2027 ballot. And a USSD interface — because a parliamentary accountability platform that requires a smartphone is a platform for the wrong people.

The entire codebase is open. Built to be forked. Built to be run by civic technologists in Uganda, Ghana, Rwanda, anywhere the same data-inaccessibility problem exists — which is everywhere."

---

### THE CONVICTION — 1:55 to 2:35
*[This is the Shuttleworth-specific section. Drop the pace further. This is what you actually believe.]*

"I want to be direct about something the fellowship asks for: what I believe that others do not.

I believe the civic technology community has been solving the wrong problem. Better information does not produce accountability. Accountability requires consequence — the structural condition in which representative behaviour has electoral cost.

I also believe that open-source is not an ethics statement. It is a survival strategy. A proprietary accountability platform can be pressured, defunded, or shut down. An open methodology that has been independently deployed in twelve countries cannot. Permanence comes from distribution, not from any single organisation's commitment to keeping the lights on.

And I believe this work needs to be done now — not because the window is closing, though it is — but because the democratic crisis of this moment is fundamentally an infrastructure crisis. The philosophy was never the problem. The feedback loop was never completed. Chanuka completes it."

---

### CLOSE — 2:35 to 3:00
*[Return to where you started. Quieter. The ending should feel like the beginning made sense.]*

"The protests last year were extraordinary. The civic energy exists. It has always existed.

What has not existed is the infrastructure that converts that energy into lasting consequence.

I am building that infrastructure. In the open. With the specific belief that democratic accountability is a solvable engineering problem — and that solving it in Kenya creates a replicable model for every parliament on the continent.

The Shuttleworth Fellowship is the runway to prove it."

*[Hold camera contact for two full seconds after the last word. Do not look away first.]*

---

## POST-SCRIPT: WHAT TO CUT IF YOU RUN LONG

The script times to exactly 3 minutes at deliberate pace. If your natural delivery runs long, cut in this order:

1. First cut: The second paragraph of THE PLATFORM section (the three components in development). Keep the opening sentence and jump to "The entire codebase is open."
2. Second cut: Shorten THE ARGUMENT section — keep only: *"The accountability loop was never completed. Transparency tools have been the wrong investment. Not transparency. Consequence."*
3. Never cut: The opening 30 seconds. The conviction section. The close.

The opening and the conviction section are what the committee will remember. Everything else is supporting structure.

## DIRECTOR'S NOTES — COMMON FAILURE MODES

**The performance problem:** Most applicants perform conviction rather than demonstrate it. The difference is visible. Conviction lives in precision — the specific number (4.3 million), the specific problem (the trojan bill), the specific belief stated plainly. Performance lives in volume, speed, and filler language ("I'm really passionate about..."). Remove every instance of "I'm passionate about," "I believe strongly that," and "incredibly important." The words carry the conviction or they do not.

**The humility trap:** Some applicants, sensing that Shuttleworth selects for openness and collaboration, over-index on "I don't have all the answers" and "this is a community effort." This reads as uncertainty about your own work. You do have answers — you have built them into operational infrastructure. Lead with what you know. The collaboration is in the open-source architecture, not in the performance of epistemic humility.

**The comparison mistake:** Do not mention other platforms — mySociety, Mzalendo, OpenParliament — in the video unless you are making a specific argument about how Chanuka is structurally different. Name-dropping comparators reads as insecurity. The committee knows the space. Let Chanuka stand on its own terms.

**The speed problem:** Founders always speak faster than they should on camera. Set a physical reminder — a dot on the camera lens, a finger-width gap between each sentence — to force the pace down. The sentences are short. The pauses do the work.
