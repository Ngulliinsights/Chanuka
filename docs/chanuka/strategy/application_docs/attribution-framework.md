# The Attribution Framework
## Disaggregating Personal Culpability, Systemic Inheritance, and External Constraint

### *How to make accountability claims that survive deflection — and make deflection itself evidence*

---

> *"The question is never whether a leader inherited constraints. Every leader inherits constraints. The question is whether the decisions they made within those constraints were competent, honest, and oriented toward public interest. That question has a specific, traceable answer."*

---

## THE DEFLECTION PROBLEM

Every accountable leader in the history of democratic governance has, at some point, deployed one of five deflection strategies:

**1. The Global Condition Deflection**
*"The cost of living crisis is a global phenomenon. Kenya is not immune to international commodity prices, dollar strength, or supply chain disruption."*
True as far as it goes. The deflection works because it is partially accurate. The accountability gap it conceals: *how did this leadership respond to those conditions*, and did that response help or harm?

**2. The Inheritance Deflection**
*"We inherited a debt burden / broken institutions / a sabotaged economy / an IMF straitjacket. What you're experiencing is the consequence of decisions made before we took office."*
Sometimes legitimate for the first year. Becomes a deflection when the same argument is made in year three, when the conditions have worsened under new policy decisions, or when peer countries with comparable inheritance have diverged positively.

**3. The Sabotage Deflection**
*"The deep state is working against us. Vested interests are undermining our reforms. The opposition is manufacturing the crisis."*
Structurally unfalsifiable — which is why it is deployed. The counter is not to deny that opposition or institutional friction exists, but to ask: if resistance is the explanation, what specific decisions were blocked, by whom, and what alternative path was attempted?

**4. The Complexity Deflection**
*"These are complex, structural, long-term problems. No government can solve them in a single term. Anyone who claims otherwise doesn't understand economics / governance / development."*
Used to make accountability claims seem naive. The counter: no one is asking for perfection. The accountability question is whether the decisions made moved conditions toward resolution or away from it.

**5. The Progress Deflection**
*"Look how far we've come. The road to State House was worse than this road. Inflation was higher in 2012. More children are in school today."*
Selective comparison. The counter is not to dispute the specific improvement cited but to ask: compared to what baseline, measured against what trajectory, and at what cost to which population?

## THE THREE RINGS OF ACCOUNTABILITY

The framework that makes deflection collapse is not arguing that nothing is inherited or structural. It is distinguishing, with precision, what belongs in each of three rings — and holding leaders accountable only for what belongs in theirs.

---

### Ring 1: The Personal Decision Ring
*"This is what you chose."*

This ring contains everything a leader explicitly decided. It is the most defensible territory for accountability claims and the hardest to deflect.

**What belongs here:**
- Every vote cast in parliament (for MPs): documented, timestamped, sourced to Hansard
- Every appointment made: who was given institutional authority and what they did with it
- Every budget priority: what was funded and what was cut, when choices were available
- Every policy response to a known condition: not the condition itself but the chosen response
- Every public statement made under oath or recorded: promises, commitments, stated priorities
- Everything done, and everything demonstrably available to be done that was not done

**Why this ring is deflection-proof:**
A vote is not inheritable. An appointment is not a global commodity price. A budget allocation is not an IMF condition — it is a choice made within constraints that still contained choices. The personal decision ring is where Chanuka's core accountability methodology operates: not on outcomes, but on documented decisions.

**The critical distinction:** Leaders are not accountable for *outcomes* in the personal decision ring. They are accountable for *decisions*. A leader who made the right decision and got a bad outcome because of genuinely uncontrollable factors deserves a different assessment than a leader who made a predictably bad decision and got a predictably bad outcome. Conflating decisions and outcomes is what makes accountability claims vulnerable. Separating them is what makes them specific and defensible.

---

### Ring 2: The Policy Environment Ring
*"This is what you set in motion."*

This ring contains the institutional and structural conditions a leader has actively shaped — not directly decided but built, incentivised, or allowed to persist within their agency window.

**What belongs here:**
- Institutional appointments that shaped the policy environment: the DPP they chose, the IEBC commissioners they influenced, the KRA leadership they installed
- Regulatory choices: what they chose to enforce, what they chose not to
- Institutional design decisions: what oversight mechanisms they strengthened or weakened
- Budget patterns across the term: not a single allocation but the direction of travel across multiple budgets
- The quality of information they maintained: did they commission credible data, or did policy operate on politically convenient numbers?

**Why this ring matters:**
Leaders rarely personally execute corruption or incompetence. They create the environment in which corruption or incompetence either faces consequence or does not. The policy environment ring is where systemic accountability lives. A leader who did not personally steal but appointed people who stole and did not act when they knew is not innocent. Their culpability is in the policy environment they created and maintained.

**The deflection this ring counters:**
The sabotage deflection most often. When a leader claims institutional friction blocked their reform agenda, the policy environment ring asks: who appointed the people creating the friction? Who had the power to create accountability for obstruction and did not use it? Who set the incentive structure that made obstruction more rewarding than compliance?

---

### Ring 3: The Structural Inheritance Ring
*"This is what you received — and what you did with it."*

This ring is where inherited conditions, global factors, and genuine structural constraints live. It is the only ring where deflection has legitimate standing — and even here, the accountability question does not disappear. It shifts.

**What belongs here:**
- Debt inherited from prior administrations with documented terms and conditions
- Global commodity prices, interest rate environments, dollar-exchange dynamics
- Constitutional or legal constraints that genuinely limited available options
- Institutional cultures that take longer than a single term to shift
- Infrastructure deficits that predate the current administration by decades
- Climate shocks, disease events, conflict spillover — external events outside domestic policy control

**Why this ring does not eliminate accountability:**
Inheriting a constraint is not the same as having no choices within it. Every constrained environment still contains decisions. The accountability questions in Ring 3 are:

1. **Did they accurately diagnose the inheritance?** A leader who misrepresented the severity of what they inherited to win an election, then cited it as explanation for failure, is accountable for the misrepresentation and for the gap between what they promised knowing the constraints and what was possible within them.

2. **Did their decisions within the constraint move toward resolution or away from it?** An inherited debt crisis under a government that added to the debt is different from one under a government that stabilised the trajectory.

3. **Did peer countries with comparable constraints diverge?** This is the counterfactual test. If Rwanda, Tanzania, and Ethiopia faced comparable global commodity price pressures in the same period and produced different fiscal outcomes, the difference between Kenya's trajectory and theirs is not attributable to the global condition they shared. It is attributable to the decisions that differed.

4. **Did they use the inherited constraint as a cover for Ring 1 decisions?** The most sophisticated deflection strategy is to use Ring 3 (genuine structural constraints) to obscure Ring 1 (personal decisions made within the same period). A government that inherits an IMF programme and then signs a new IMF agreement with worse conditionality has moved a Ring 3 condition into Ring 1 territory. The original inheritance is legitimate. The new agreement is a decision.

## THE DECISION TRACE — A FORENSIC METHODOLOGY

For any specific harm, the attribution framework requires tracing backward through five specific questions before any accountability claim is made. Skipping any question leaves the claim vulnerable.

**Question 1: What is the specific, measurable harm?**
Not "the economy is bad" — that is a narrative. The traceable harm is specific: "The fuel levy introduced in the Finance Bill 2023 increased the average household's monthly transport cost by KSh 2,400 in Nairobi's low-income constituencies." A specific harm has a specific magnitude, a specific affected population, and a specific timeline.

**Question 2: What decision produced it?**
Every specific harm has an upstream decision chain. The fuel levy has a Bill. The Bill has a vote. The vote has named MPs. The MPs have a Hansard record. This is where accountability becomes documentable — not at the level of the harm but at the level of the decision that produced it.

**Question 3: What alternatives were available at the time of the decision?**
This is the counterfactual question that separates constrained decision-making from personal failure. If the only alternative to the fuel levy was defaulting on sovereign debt, the constraint was genuine. If the alternative was a windfall tax on large corporates that was available and rejected, the choice to impose the levy is a personal decision dressed as necessity.

**Question 4: What did the decision-maker know, and when?**
The information test. A bad decision made without available information is a different kind of failure from a bad decision made against available evidence. Civil society organisations had published fiscal impact analyses of the Finance Bill provisions before the vote. MPs who voted for it after those analyses were public cannot claim information deficit. MPs who voted after requesting and reading the analyses and proceeding anyway have the clearest culpability.

**Question 5: What was the decision-maker's response when the harm became visible?**
The trajectory after the decision is as important as the decision itself. A leader who made a bad decision, recognised the harm, and corrected course demonstrates a different quality of governance from one who defended, doubled down, or denied. The response is itself a decision in the personal ring.

## THE AGENCY WINDOW — TIME AS ACCOUNTABILITY EVIDENCE

One of the most important and least used accountability tools is temporal analysis: how long has this leader held power, and what has happened to the inherited condition during that time?

**The principle:** The legitimacy of inheritance deflection decays with time and with the leader's own decisions.

A leader six months into their term can legitimately cite what they inherited. A leader four years into their term, who has made budget decisions, appointed officials, passed legislation, and shaped the policy environment — and who is still citing their inheritance for conditions that have worsened — is not citing an inheritance. They are citing evidence of their own failure to govern effectively within the constraints they held.

**The specific test:** For any condition a leader claims to have inherited, ask:
- Has it improved, stabilised, or worsened during their tenure?
- What specific decisions did they make about it?
- Did those decisions have a predictable direction? Did the trajectory follow the prediction?
- If the condition worsened, what decisions made under this government contributed to the worsening?

If the inherited condition has worsened, and the decisions that were made during the incumbent's tenure contributed to that worsening, the inheritance deflection has expired. The worsening portion belongs in Ring 1.

## THE DEFLECTION COUNTER-MATRIX

Once the three-ring framework is applied and the decision trace completed, each deflection strategy has a specific, evidence-based counter.

| Deflection | What It Concedes | The Counter |
|---|---|---|
| "Global conditions" | That the external factor exists | "Tanzania and Rwanda faced the same commodity prices. Their fiscal trajectory differed. What decisions differed?" |
| "We inherited this" | That a prior condition existed | "What specific decisions did you make about it, and what direction did those decisions move the condition?" |
| "We're being sabotaged" | That friction exists | "Who appointed the people creating the friction? Who had removal authority and did not use it?" |
| "It's complex" | Nothing — complexity is universal | "No one asked for a simple solution. We asked whether your decisions moved toward or away from resolution." |
| "Look at our progress" | That some things improved | "Compared to what trajectory? At what cost? Which population received the benefit, which bore the cost?" |

## THE VOTE AS THE CLEANEST ACCOUNTABILITY UNIT

For Chanuka's specific context — parliamentary MP accountability — the vote is the analytical unit that bypasses almost every deflection strategy simultaneously.

A vote:
- Is a direct personal decision (Ring 1, unambiguously)
- Is not inheritable — the MP was present and made the decision
- Is not attributable to global conditions
- Is documented with date, division record, and MP name
- Was made with available information at the time
- Represents a specific choice between available options

This is why Chanuka's core methodology operates on voting records rather than outcomes. The Finance Bill levy may have had global commodity price context. The vote to pass it did not. The MP who voted for it made a documented decision that is traceable, attributable, and defensible against every standard deflection strategy.

The one deflection available to an MP on a vote is party discipline: they voted with their party under pressure. The counter: Kenya's constitution explicitly grants MPs a mandate to represent their constituency. Party discipline is a political reality. It is not a legal immunity from accountability. An MP who voted against constituency interest under party pressure is accountable for that vote and for their choice to prioritise party over constituency.

## THE SUMMARY FRAMEWORK

A defensible accountability claim has three components:

**1. Attribution is complete.** The harm has been disaggregated across the three rings. What belongs to global conditions is conceded. What belongs to inheritance is identified and bounded by time and trajectory. What belongs to personal decision is isolated and documented.

**2. The decision is traced, not the outcome.** The accountability claim is about a specific decision, by a specific person, at a specific time, made with available information, when alternatives existed.

**3. The deflection is anticipated.** For every accountability claim, the likely deflection strategy has been identified, the counter-argument prepared, and the evidence assembled. A claim that cannot survive its most likely challenge has not been tested enough.

When these three components are present, the accountability claim is not just morally compelling. It is analytically specific, evidentially grounded, and rhetorically durable. The leader who deflects against it is not demonstrating their innocence. They are demonstrating which questions they cannot answer.
