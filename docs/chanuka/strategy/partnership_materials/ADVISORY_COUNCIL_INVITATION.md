## What Chanuka Is

Chanuka is a parliamentary accountability platform for Kenya. It ingests parliamentary data, translates legislation into plain language, detects hidden provisions in bills, measures the gap between how MPs vote and what their constituents want, and converts that gap into electoral pressure at the constituency level.

The platform is built on a theory of change that is simple and largely untested in East Africa: that democracy functions not when citizens have more information, but when representative behaviour has measurable electoral consequences. Most civic technology addresses the information gap. Chanuka addresses the accountability gap — the step between knowing what happened and being able to act on it.

The platform is in development. A Nairobi pilot covering 17 constituencies and 4.3 million constituents is the first milestone. The full stack — scraping pipeline, accountability scorecard, constitutional violation detection, plain-language translation, and USSD access for non-smartphone users — is built and being hardened for production.

It is built solo. That is relevant context for what an advisory council means at this stage.

## Why Now

We are at an inflection point that gives this work urgency beyond Kenya.

The retreat of established democracies from their own accountability norms — most visibly in the United States, but not only there — has sent a signal to governments across the developing world: democratic form can persist without democratic function, and the cost of that substitution is lower than previously assumed. Already-compromised governments are reading that signal and adjusting their behaviour. The institutions assumed to enforce accountability have demonstrated, in real time, that they cannot always do so.

The response cannot be to defend democratic aesthetics — the procedures, the forms, the rituals that persist after their functional content has been hollowed out. The response must be to complete democratic infrastructure: to build the systems that make accountability real rather than procedural.

Chanuka is one version of that response, built for Kenya, designed for the conditions democracy actually faces in 2026. An advisory council formed now is a council that helps determine whether that response is adequate to the moment.

## What the Advisory Council Is

The council has no formal governance role. It is not a board. It does not carry fiduciary responsibility. What it does:

**Provides strategic guidance.** Occasional conversations — two or three times a year, individually or as a group — about direction, framing, and the decisions that matter. No standing agenda, no mandatory attendance, no formal outputs required.

**Lends credibility at a critical moment.** In the landscape of Kenyan and African civic technology, association with this work signals something about its seriousness and its values. That signal matters most now, before the pilot, when the platform is being evaluated by funders, potential partners, and civil society organisations.

**Opens networks deliberately.** Not as a transaction — "introduce me to X" — but as a considered orientation: advisors who understand what Chanuka is trying to do, and who can, in their own judgment, surface relevant people, conversations, or opportunities when they encounter them.

**Shapes the platform's ethical commitments.** The decisions being made now — about data confidence levels, about USSD access, about how accountability scores are displayed and by whom — are decisions with political and ethical dimensions. Advisors who hold expertise in those dimensions make those decisions better.

## The Profiles Being Sought

The council is not being assembled to look complete. It is being assembled to be useful. The gaps that matter most right now are:

**Constitutional and legal depth.** Someone who understands Kenya's constitutional architecture — not as a credential, but as a living question: what does accountability actually mean within the specific constraints of the Constitution of Kenya 2010, and where are the limits of what a platform like Chanuka can and should do?

**Civic society credibility in Nairobi.** Someone with established relationships in the constituencies the pilot will serve — not to validate the platform, but to ground it. Accountability infrastructure that is designed without the people it is designed for is just another form of form without function.

**International democracy and governance networks.** Someone whose connections span the organisations — funders, research institutions, democracy practitioners — that this work needs to reach. Not as a door-opener but as a person whose judgment about which doors matter is itself the value.

**Journalism and media.** Someone who understands how accountability information travels — how a scorecard becomes a story, how a story changes electoral behaviour, and what the difference is between civic technology that informs and civic technology that shifts power.

**African civic technology.** Someone who has built or operated civic technology at scale in Africa, who understands what parliament.go.ke's broken infrastructure actually means in practice, and who can speak to what has been tried and what has failed.

**Cultural and artistic practice.** Someone working at the intersection of culture, technology, and civic life — who understands that how a platform presents accountability data is itself a cultural and political act, and that the design of democratic infrastructure is inseparable from the values it encodes.

## What Is Being Asked

The ask is direct: join the advisory council, or identify someone in your network who should.

If you join: a conversation in the next four weeks to understand where the platform is, what the pilot will do, and where your perspective would be most useful. After that, contact when it matters — not on a schedule.

There is no compensation at this stage. The honest offer is association with work that is serious about a problem that is urgent, at the moment when association means something — before the pilot, before the platform has an audience, before the questions it is asking have been answered.

## The Longer Arc

Chanuka's ambition does not end with Nairobi. The accountability gap it is built to close is not a Nairobi problem or a Kenya problem. The parliamentary data infrastructure it has built — the scraping pipeline, the gap calculation methodology, the scorecard architecture — is designed to be portable to any parliamentary democracy where voting records are technically public and practically inaccessible. That includes most of Sub-Saharan Africa, and several democracies beyond it.

The advisory council being formed now is the beginning of the network that makes that arc possible. The people who help determine what Chanuka becomes in Nairobi are the people who will understand, better than anyone, what it could become across the continent.
