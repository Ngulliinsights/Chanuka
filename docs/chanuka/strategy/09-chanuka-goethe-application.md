## SECTION 1 — ARTISTIC PRACTICE AND PROFESSIONAL BACKGROUND

### Statement of Practice

My practice sits at the intersection of civic architecture, media art, and democratic philosophy. I design systems — digital, conceptual, and spatial — that make invisible political structures visible and consequential.

The work that defines my practice is Chanuka: a parliamentary accountability platform for Kenya that I have been building since [year]. Chanuka is not, at its core, a technology project. It is an act of civic design — the construction of a system whose purpose is to make the gap between democratic promise and democratic reality perceptible to the people that gap most affects.

The platform tracks parliamentary legislation, detects hidden provisions in bills, translates law into plain language, measures the misalignment between how elected representatives vote and what their constituents want, and surfaces that misalignment as an accountability score at the constituency level. It includes a USSD interface for citizens without smartphones. It incorporates a Maasai-influenced visual identity. It carries, in the same repository as its database migrations, a collection of poems, philosophical essays, and a manifesto.

Those documents — `chanuka_final_poems.md`, `manifesto.md`, `sustainable_uprising.md`, `philosophical_connections_analysis.md`, `Scriptural Distributed Leadership.md`, `ezra-nehemiah-chanuka.md` — are not supplementary material. They are the soul documents of the project. The Ezra-Nehemiah framework that runs through the platform's design philosophy draws on the Hebrew biblical account of a people rebuilding civic infrastructure after collapse — walls, governance systems, and collective identity simultaneously — as a model for what democratic reconstruction looks like in a post-colonial context. The poems articulate what the data cannot: the emotional texture of living in a democracy that performs its own rituals while its accountability mechanism remains unbuilt.

This is design as cultural practice. The platform's architecture — the decision to build for feature phones, to assign confidence levels to every data point, to refuse to display uncertain information publicly — encodes a set of values about who democracy is for and what honesty in civic infrastructure looks like. These are aesthetic decisions as much as technical ones. They answer the question that every designer must answer: *who is allowed into this building, and on what terms?*

My broader practice includes the philosophical and textual work that informs the platform's design. I work across writing, systems design, and visual identity, treating the construction of civic infrastructure as a form of cultural production — one that is accountable to the people it serves in the same way that a building is accountable to the people who inhabit it.

## SECTION 2 — MOBILITY PURPOSE AND CULTURAL EXCHANGE RATIONALE

### The Cross-Cultural Question This Mobility Will Address

The question at the centre of this mobility project is one that cannot be answered from Nairobi alone: *What does it look like when a society rebuilds democratic infrastructure after its institutional foundations have been delegitimised?*

Germany has answered this question. Kenya is beginning to.

Germany's post-war democratic reconstruction — the Grundgesetz (Basic Law) of 1949, the Federal Constitutional Court, the culture of Verfassungspatriotismus (constitutional patriotism) developed by Habermas and others, the deliberate institutional architecture designed to prevent a return to fascism — is the most thoroughly documented case of a society rebuilding democratic infrastructure from first principles. The designers of the Grundgesetz were not restoring a previous system. They were designing a new one, with full awareness of how the previous one had been captured and destroyed.

Kenya's position in 2026 is structurally different but philosophically adjacent. Kenya did not emerge from fascism. It emerged from colonialism — a system that imposed democratic form (elections, parliaments, constitutions) without democratic function (accountability, representation, consequence). The institutional templates inherited at independence were designed for control, not for accountability. The accountability infrastructure was never built. What is being built now — by platforms like Chanuka, by civil society organisations, by the citizens who participated in the constitutional referendum of 2010 — is a first-principles reconstruction of what democratic function actually requires.

The cross-cultural exchange this mobility enables is a sustained dialogue between these two reconstruction projects: Germany's post-fascist democratic architecture and Kenya's post-colonial democratic construction. The questions they are answering are not identical. The design principles they have developed — the institutional safeguards, the cultural practices, the artistic and philosophical frameworks for processing democratic failure — are enormously relevant to each other.

### Artistic and Cultural Dimensions of the Exchange

The exchange is not primarily technical. It is cultural and artistic in three specific registers.

**First: civic architecture as cultural practice.** Germany has a rich tradition of thinking about the design of democratic space — literally and metaphorically. The Reichstag's glass dome, designed by Norman Foster, is one of the most discussed examples of architecture as democratic statement: transparency made structural, the public literally able to look down on its representatives. The Topography of Terror, built on the ruins of the Gestapo headquarters, is a different example: accountability made spatial, the physical record of institutional failure embedded in the city's geography. The cultural question these buildings ask — *what does accountability look like as architecture?* — is the same question Chanuka asks in digital form. This mobility creates the conditions for a direct dialogue between these traditions.

**Second: the aesthetics of democratic failure.** German cultural production has spent eighty years processing the aesthetics of institutional collapse — in literature, film, visual art, and philosophy. Brecht, Sebald, Richter, Habermas: the artistic language developed in Germany for understanding how a society accepts the corruption of its democratic forms, and what reconstruction feels like from the inside, is among the most sophisticated in the world. This tradition is almost entirely absent from the Kenyan civic discourse — not because the experience is absent, but because the cultural frameworks for articulating it have not been translated across the post-colonial context. This mobility is an act of translation: bringing those frameworks into contact with the Kenyan reconstruction project, and returning with a language for what Chanuka is doing that is richer than the vocabulary of civic technology.

**Third: co-creation of cross-cultural civic design methodology.** The concrete output of this mobility is a collaborative design document: a methodology for building civic accountability infrastructure that draws on both the German post-war institutional design tradition and the Kenyan post-colonial reconstruction context. This document would be the first systematic attempt to bring these two traditions into dialogue, and would be made available as an open resource for civic technologists, designers, and cultural practitioners working on democratic infrastructure across Sub-Saharan Africa.

## SECTION 3 — PROJECT DESCRIPTION

### Project Title
*Rebuilding the House: Democratic Infrastructure Design Across Post-Fascist and Post-Colonial Contexts*

### Objectives Addressed
**Explore** — Investigating the German tradition of post-fascist democratic reconstruction as a cultural and design resource for Kenyan post-colonial democratic infrastructure building.
**Connect** — Building sustained relationships between Kenyan civic technology practitioners and German cultural institutions, civic designers, and democratic philosophy practitioners.

### Proposed Destination
Berlin, Germany — with possible extension to Munich (Goethe-Institut headquarters and related civic cultural institutions).

Berlin is the right destination for three reasons. First, it is the city in which Germany's democratic reconstruction was most visibly performed — the Reichstag, the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe, the Topography of Terror, the Berlin Wall documentation centres are all here, and together they constitute the world's most extensive architectural and cultural record of democratic failure and reconstruction. Second, it is the centre of Germany's civic technology and democratic innovation community — organisations including Wikimedia DE, Open Knowledge Foundation Germany, Prototype Fund, and AlgorithmWatch are based here and working on questions directly relevant to Chanuka's design. Third, the Goethe-Institut's headquarters provides an institutional bridge between the artistic and civic dimensions of the exchange.

### Proposed Partner Institution
**[To be confirmed before June 15 submission]**

Target partners (approach in this order):

**Primary target: Open Knowledge Foundation Germany (OKF DE), Berlin**
OKF DE runs Prototype Fund — Germany's primary public funding mechanism for civic technology — and has deep expertise in parliamentary transparency infrastructure (they built and maintain FragDenStaat, Germany's freedom of information platform, and Offenes Parlament, the parliamentary monitoring tool). A partnership with OKF DE would provide direct dialogue between the Chanuka model and the most developed civic technology infrastructure in Germany.

**Secondary target: Goethe-Institut Nairobi / Goethe-Institut Berlin**
A partnership facilitated by the Goethe-Institut itself, connecting Chanuka's development work to the Institut's existing Africa-Europe cultural exchange programme. This would position the mobility as explicitly bilateral — not just a Kenyan practitioner visiting Germany, but the beginning of a cultural exchange between Kenyan civic design practice and German democratic culture institutions.

**Tertiary target: Technische Universität Berlin — Centre for Technology and Society**
TU Berlin's Centre for Technology and Society works on the cultural and ethical dimensions of technology design. A research residency component would support the methodology document output.

### Activities During Mobility (Proposed 4-week visit)

**Week 1 — Architectural and cultural research.**
Visiting and studying the key sites of Germany's democratic accountability architecture: the Reichstag public access programme, the Topography of Terror, the German Democratic Republic documentation centres, the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe. The research question: what design decisions were made, consciously or unconsciously, that encode specific values about accountability, memory, and democratic reconstruction?

**Week 2 — Civic technology dialogue.**
Working sessions at OKF DE and related organisations. Presenting Chanuka's design methodology and the post-colonial reconstruction context it responds to. Learning from the design decisions made in building FragDenStaat and Offenes Parlament — the assumptions encoded in those platforms, the constraints of the German institutional context, and what translates and what does not.

**Week 3 — Artistic and philosophical exchange.**
Engagement with German cultural practitioners working at the intersection of civic life and artistic practice. Specifically: artists working on democratic memory and institutional accountability, designers working on civic space, and philosophers working in the Habermas tradition of constitutional patriotism and its implications for non-Western democratic reconstruction.

**Week 4 — Synthesis and co-creation.**
Working with the partner institution to produce the first draft of the cross-cultural civic design methodology document. Public presentation of the work-in-progress at a Berlin civic culture event — presenting the Kenyan post-colonial reconstruction context to a German audience, and what Chanuka has learned from the German post-fascist tradition.

### Outputs

**Primary output:** *Rebuilding the House* — a cross-cultural civic design methodology document (15,000–20,000 words) bringing the German post-fascist democratic design tradition into dialogue with Kenyan post-colonial democratic reconstruction. Freely available under Creative Commons licence. Submitted for publication in a peer-reviewed democracy or design studies journal.

**Secondary output:** A public presentation in Berlin documenting the Kenyan accountability infrastructure context and Chanuka's design approach, recorded and made available in English, Swahili, and German.

**Tertiary output:** A sustained partnership with at least one German civic technology or cultural institution, formalised as a memorandum of understanding, enabling ongoing exchange between the Kenyan and German civic design communities.

## SECTION 4 — CROSS-CULTURAL AND ARTISTIC SIGNIFICANCE

### Why This Exchange Cannot Happen Remotely

The argument for this methodology document can be made in writing. What cannot be made in writing is the embodied cultural experience of encountering Germany's democratic reconstruction architecture in person — standing in the Reichstag's glass dome and understanding, physically, what it means to build accountability into structure; visiting the Topography of Terror and understanding what it means to make institutional failure permanently visible; sitting with OKF DE's practitioners and understanding not just what they built but why, and what it cost.

The methodology document that comes out of this mobility will be different — richer, more grounded, more culturally specific — than anything that could be produced without the travel. That difference is the case for mobility.

### The Significance for African Civic Cultural Practice

The German tradition of processing democratic failure through culture — through architecture, literature, philosophy, and institutional design — has not been systematically engaged with by African civic practitioners. This is partly a language barrier, partly a colonial knowledge hierarchy that directs intellectual traffic from North to South rather than permitting genuine exchange. The exchange this mobility enables is a deliberate act of intellectual sovereignty: bringing two first-principles reconstruction projects into dialogue as equals, each with something to learn from and to offer the other.

The output of this exchange — the methodology document, the public presentation, the sustained partnership — will be a resource not just for Chanuka but for every civic technology practitioner, designer, and cultural worker across Sub-Saharan Africa who is building democratic infrastructure under conditions that the existing literature was not written to address.

### The Significance for German Civic Culture

The German experience of post-fascist reconstruction is, in important ways, exhaustively documented — and in other ways, strangely provincial. The frameworks developed for understanding how a society rebuilds democratic accountability after institutional failure have rarely been tested against the post-colonial reconstruction context, where the failure was not internal collapse but external imposition. Bringing the Kenyan reconstruction project into conversation with the German tradition creates new questions for the German tradition: What do the design principles of the Grundgesetz assume about the institutional baseline from which reconstruction begins? What translates to a context where the institutions themselves were tools of control? What is universal in the German experience and what is specific to the European post-fascist context?

These questions make the German tradition richer. The exchange goes both ways.
