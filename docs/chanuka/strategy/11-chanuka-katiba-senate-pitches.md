# PITCH ONE — KATIBA INSTITUTE
## A Partnership Proposal

---

## Context Before You Read This

Katiba Institute's 2025–2029 strategic plan, launched in July 2025, describes a "systems-oriented approach designed to achieve sustainable, long-term change through multi-level and collaborative interventions." Chanuka is a system. This proposal is about what happens when constitutional expertise and accountability infrastructure work on the same problem from different sides.

This is not a request for legal validation. It is a proposal for a working relationship between two organisations whose work is incomplete without the other's.

---

## The Shared Problem, Named Precisely

Katiba Institute's work is anchored in a conviction stated explicitly in its founding documents: *"The Constitution is the supreme law of the land and must be treated as such."*

Chanuka's work is anchored in a different but adjacent conviction: the Constitution of Kenya 2010 contains the most comprehensive framework for representative accountability in East Africa — and almost none of its accountability mechanisms are operationally enforced by the institutions designed to enforce them.

Article 1(1): all sovereign power belongs to the people. Article 94(1): legislative authority is derived from the people. Article 118(1): parliament shall conduct its business in an open manner and facilitate public participation. These are not aspirations. They are constitutional obligations. The gap between those obligations and the daily reality of Kenyan parliamentary life — voting records inaccessible to constituents, bills passed without genuine public participation, provisions inserted into legislation in ways that obscure their constitutional implications — is the gap both organisations are working to close.

Katiba Institute closes it through litigation and research. Chanuka closes it through infrastructure. The question this proposal puts on the table is whether those two approaches, coordinated, close it faster and more durably than either can alone.

---

## What Katiba Institute Has That Chanuka Needs

**Constitutional interpretation depth.** Chanuka's constitutional violation detection is methodologically sound as a data pipeline — it can match bill provisions against the text and structure of the Constitution 2010. What it cannot do without human constitutional expertise is interpret ambiguous provisions, weigh competing constitutional values, or assess whether a specific legislative drafting technique constitutes a genuine constitutional violation or an aggressive but permissible exercise of parliamentary discretion. That distinction requires the kind of constitutional literacy that Katiba Institute has built since 2011.

**Strategic litigation intelligence.** Katiba Institute has litigated against the Finance Bill 2024 protest crackdowns, the Constitutional Amendment Bill 2025, and numerous instances of parliamentary procedure that violated public participation requirements under Articles 10 and 118. Each of those cases was preceded by a legislative act — a bill passed, a provision inserted, a process truncated — that Chanuka's detection methodology is designed to flag *before* litigation becomes necessary. Katiba Institute knows, better than anyone, what the pattern of constitutional violation looks like in Kenyan parliamentary practice. That pattern knowledge should be encoded in Chanuka's detection methodology.

**Public credibility on constitutional questions.** When Chanuka's platform flags a bill as potentially containing provisions that violate the Constitution, the flag needs to be credible to citizens, journalists, and civil society organisations. A flag endorsed by or developed in collaboration with Katiba Institute carries a weight that a flag from an independent civic technology platform does not. The credibility is not decoration — it is what makes the flag consequential rather than algorithmic noise.

**The 2025–2029 strategic plan's "systems" framing.** The new strategy explicitly describes a systems-oriented approach to constitutional change. Chanuka is the feedback loop that connects constitutional violations in legislation to citizen awareness to electoral consequence. That feedback loop is a constitutional accountability system. The partnership being proposed here is a component of the system Katiba Institute's new strategy describes.

---

## What Chanuka Offers Katiba Institute

**Early warning infrastructure.** Katiba Institute currently identifies constitutional violations through legal analysis of passed or nearly-passed legislation. Chanuka's detection methodology — scanning bill provisions systematically against constitutional provisions as bills are introduced — creates the possibility of flagging constitutional concerns at the First Reading stage, before the bill has completed its parliamentary journey. Earlier flags mean more time for public participation interventions, for legal analysis, and for the kind of constitutional advocacy that Katiba Institute does before litigation becomes necessary.

**Constituency-level reach for civic education.** Katiba Institute's civic education work reaches citizens through workshops, publications, and media. Chanuka's platform reaches citizens at the constituency level with specific, concrete information about how their MP voted on bills with constitutional implications — translated into plain language. The combination of Katiba Institute's constitutional depth and Chanuka's distribution infrastructure creates civic education at a scale and specificity that neither achieves independently.

**Data on the pattern of constitutional evasion.** Over time, Chanuka's detection logs will constitute a dataset of legislative provisions that were flagged as constitutionally concerning — across bills, sessions, and parliaments. This dataset has research value for Katiba Institute's publications work and strategic intelligence value for its litigation practice: it reveals the patterns and techniques by which constitutional obligations are routinely evaded in Kenyan parliamentary practice.

**Evidence base for public participation failures.** Under Articles 10 and 118, bills must be subjected to genuine public participation. Chanuka tracks bill timelines — introduction dates, reading dates, committee referral periods. This data can identify bills whose parliamentary journey was too compressed to permit genuine public participation, producing an evidence base directly relevant to Katiba Institute's litigation on public participation violations.

---

## The Specific Partnership Being Proposed

Not a Memorandum of Understanding on day one. A working relationship with three concrete components:

**Component 1 — Constitutional methodology review.**
Katiba Institute reviews Chanuka's constitutional violation detection methodology — the framework for matching bill provisions against specific constitutional articles — and advises on its constitutional soundness. This is a bounded, one-time engagement: two or three sessions with a constitutional lawyer reviewing the detection logic and advising on where it is legally rigorous and where it overreaches. The output is a methodology that Katiba Institute has reviewed and is comfortable being associated with.

**Component 2 — Bill flagging collaboration.**
During the Nairobi pilot, when Chanuka's detection flags a bill provision as potentially constitutionally concerning, that flag is shared with Katiba Institute before it is published publicly. Katiba Institute has 48 hours to review and either endorse, refine, or withdraw the flag. Flags that Katiba Institute reviews and endorses carry a clear attribution. Flags that Katiba Institute withdraws are removed from public display. This creates a quality control mechanism for constitutional flags and a monitoring intelligence feed for Katiba Institute.

**Component 3 — Joint publication.**
At the end of the pilot period, Chanuka and Katiba Institute co-author a report on constitutional compliance in the 13th Parliament's Nairobi-affecting legislation: the bills flagged, the constitutional provisions implicated, the pattern of evasion identified, and the recommendations for both legislative practice and citizen advocacy. This publication serves Katiba Institute's research mandate and Chanuka's credibility with funders and civil society partners.

---

## What This Is Not

This proposal is not asking Katiba Institute to endorse Chanuka's platform, to take responsibility for its data, or to become involved in its operational management. The partnership is advisory and collaborative — Katiba Institute's constitutional expertise informs and quality-controls the constitutional dimension of Chanuka's work, and Chanuka's infrastructure serves Katiba Institute's civic education and early-warning goals. Each organisation remains independently responsible for its own work.

# PITCH TWO — CONSTITUTIONAL LAWYER, KENYAN SENATE
## An Advisory Role Conversation

---

## A Note on How This Conversation Works

This pitch is written for someone who will read it carefully and critically. You are a constitutional lawyer. You will identify the conflict of interest question before it is raised. The purpose of this document is to raise it first, answer it thoroughly, and then make the substantive case for why the advisory role being proposed is worth considering despite the institutional sensitivity.

If the conflict of interest analysis is not satisfying, the conversation ends here and no harm is done. If it is satisfying, what follows is a proposal for a contribution that is both professionally significant and — for the reasons this document will explain — genuinely important to the constitutional architecture of the country whose laws you are working within.

---

## The Conflict of Interest Question — Raised Directly

You are employed as a constitutional lawyer within the Kenyan Senate. The Senate is one of the two chambers of Parliament whose members and legislative activity Chanuka monitors. An advisory role on a platform that publishes accountability scorecards for Senators and flags Senate legislation for constitutional concerns raises a genuine question: does that role create a conflict between your professional obligations to your employer and your participation in a platform that scrutinises that employer's work?

The answer is no, for four specific reasons. Each needs to be examined rather than asserted.

**Reason 1: Chanuka uses exclusively public data.**

Every data point on Chanuka's platform is sourced from publicly available documents: Hansard records, official bill publications, and parliamentary Votes and Proceedings. Chanuka does not access, solicit, or use any information that is not already in the public domain. Your advisory role would be to advise on the constitutional interpretation framework and the methodology for flagging constitutional concerns — not to provide any information about Senate proceedings, deliberations, internal processes, or anything else that is not already published.

The test for conflict of interest in a role like this is not whether the platform involves your employer — it is whether your participation in the platform requires you to use information or access that belongs to your employer, or to act against your employer's legitimate institutional interests. Advising on constitutional interpretation methodology, using publicly available constitutional law, meets neither criterion.

**Reason 2: The constitutional analysis is non-partisan and non-electoral.**

Chanuka's constitutional violation detection flags provisions in bills against specific constitutional articles. The analysis is the same regardless of which Senator sponsored the bill, which party it serves, or what the political implications of the flag are. A flag that a bill provision potentially violates Article 118(1)'s public participation requirement is a constitutional analysis, not a political position. You would be advising on the soundness of that constitutional analysis, not on its political implications.

Your professional role as a constitutional lawyer within the Senate presumably requires you to advise on constitutional compliance as a matter of professional obligation, without regard to political outcomes. The advisory role being proposed here applies the same standard.

**Reason 3: The role is advisory, not operational.**

You would not be involved in the day-to-day operation of the platform, in decisions about what to publish or when, in the design of the accountability scorecard, or in any activity that involves evaluating the performance of specific Senators. Your role is methodological: reviewing and advising on the constitutional framework that underpins the platform's detection methodology, and — if you are comfortable with it — being available for periodic consultation on constitutional interpretation questions as they arise in the analysis of specific bills.

You would have no decision-making authority over the platform's outputs. If the platform publishes a constitutional flag you believe is methodologically unsound, your remedy is to advise that it be corrected — not to direct that it be removed or suppressed. The distinction matters: an advisory role that involves methodological review is categorically different from one that involves editorial control over published content about your employer.

**Reason 4: The platform's constitutional scrutiny is an expression of values the Senate formally endorses.**

The Senate's own mandate, under Article 96 of the Constitution, includes the protection of devolution, the review of legislation affecting counties, and the general oversight of national legislation. The constitutional obligations the platform scrutinises — public participation under Article 118, the Bill of Rights under Chapter Four, the accountability provisions of Article 10 — are obligations the Senate formally acknowledges and is institutionally required to uphold.

Advising on a platform that monitors compliance with those obligations is not working against the Senate — it is working toward the constitutional standard the Senate formally espouses. If there is a genuine conflict of interest, it runs the other way: it would be a conflict of your professional obligations as a constitutional lawyer to *decline* to contribute to a mechanism that strengthens constitutional compliance in the institution you serve.

---

## What the Advisory Role Actually Involves

The question of conflict of interest is prior to the question of what you would actually do. Having addressed it, here is what the role involves in practice.

**Constitutional methodology review (one-time, bounded).**
Review Chanuka's framework for matching bill provisions against constitutional articles. The framework exists — it is the platform's current detection methodology, developed by someone who is not a constitutional lawyer. The question you would be answering is: is this framework constitutionally sound? Where does it correctly identify the constitutional provisions at stake? Where does it overreach, conflate different constitutional obligations, or miss a more precise constitutional framing? This is a technical legal review, not a political evaluation.

**Periodic consultation on constitutional interpretation questions.**
As the platform processes specific bills and flags specific provisions, constitutional interpretation questions arise that are not resolved by the text of the Constitution alone. Questions about the constitutional distinction between substantive and procedural violations of public participation requirements. Questions about when a bill's stated purpose diverges sufficiently from its operative provisions to constitute a constitutional concern rather than a legislative policy choice. Questions about how to present constitutional uncertainty to a non-specialist audience without misrepresenting the legal position. These are questions a constitutional lawyer answers routinely. They are the questions the advisory role is designed to address.

**No involvement in accountability scoring.**
The MP and Senator accountability scorecards — the gap scores, the electoral pressure tools, the advocacy coordination features — are not within the scope of this advisory role. Your expertise is in constitutional law. The advisory role is scoped to the constitutional dimension of the platform's work and nothing else. The accountability scoring methodology is a separate system, developed independently, and would not be part of this conversation.

---

## Why This Matters

The platform will flag constitutional concerns in legislation regardless of whether a constitutional lawyer advises on its methodology. The flags will be more or less accurate, more or less legally sound, depending on the quality of the constitutional framework underlying the detection. A platform that flags constitutional violations inaccurately does not strengthen constitutional accountability — it introduces noise into the constitutional discourse and gives those with an interest in dismissing constitutional concerns an easy target.

The advisory role being proposed is, at its core, an investment in the accuracy and credibility of constitutional analysis that reaches citizens who do not have access to a constitutional lawyer. The citizens in Kibra and Starehe who will use this platform to understand what their MP voted for do not have access to someone who can explain to them why a specific bill provision raises a concern under Article 118. The platform is the mechanism by which that constitutional knowledge travels from specialists to constituents.

You have that knowledge. The question this proposal puts to you is whether you are willing to contribute it to a mechanism designed to make it accessible to the people the Constitution was written to protect.

---

## The Practical Ask

A single meeting, 45 minutes, to discuss whether the conflict of interest analysis above is satisfying and whether the methodological review described is something you would be willing to undertake. If the conflict of interest analysis is not satisfying, the conversation ends there. If it is, the next step is a review of the detection methodology — a document you can read in an hour — and a follow-up conversation about what you would recommend changing.

There is no compensation at this stage. The offer is association with work that takes the Constitution seriously as an instrument of democratic accountability, at a moment when that seriousness is both rare and necessary.
