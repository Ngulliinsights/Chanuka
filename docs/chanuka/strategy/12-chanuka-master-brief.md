## A NOTE ON HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT

Chanuka has been described in three different registers depending on the room:

- As **democratic infrastructure** — for civil society, donors, and fellowship programmes
- As **accountability architecture** — for constitutional lawyers, academic institutions, and parliamentary monitoring organisations
- As **compliance infrastructure** — for Parliament's institutional risk, civil society organisations, and research bodies

These are not three different pitches for three different products. They are three true descriptions of the same platform, each foregrounding the dimension most relevant to a specific audience. The platform does not change. The vocabulary does.

The argument that runs through all three: **Kenya's Constitution 2010 promises accountability that its institutional infrastructure cannot deliver, and Chanuka is the system built to close that gap.**

## THE PROBLEM — NAMED AT THREE DEPTHS

### At the surface: a civic access failure

Parliamentary voting records are technically public. Bills are published. Hansards are produced. An MP's voting record on the Finance Bill exists somewhere in a PDF. A citizen in Kibra cannot find it, read it, or use it. The information is public in the legal sense and inaccessible in every practical sense.

This is the surface description of the problem. It is true. It is insufficient on its own.

### One layer down: a constitutional compliance failure

Kenya's Constitution 2010 is one of the most progressive democratic frameworks in Africa. Article 1(1) vests sovereign power in the people. Article 94(1) derives legislative authority from the people. Article 118(1) requires parliament to conduct its business in an open manner and facilitate genuine public participation. Article 10 mandates transparency and accountability as national values binding on all state organs.

These are not aspirations. They are binding constitutional obligations. The Finance Act 2023 was nullified by Kenya's courts specifically because genuine public participation did not occur. The Privatisation Act 2023 met the same fate. These nullifications are not accidents — they are the judiciary enforcing what the legislature's current systems cannot operationally deliver.

The Clerk of the National Assembly and the Clerk of the Senate face personal liability — contempt of court, career consequences, audit scrutiny — for failures of parliamentary procedure. Their current systems have no answer to this risk.

### At the foundation: a democratic infrastructure failure

Representative democracy at scale rests on a feedback loop: citizens express preferences through elections, representatives translate those preferences into legislation, and citizens evaluate the translation and adjust their electoral behaviour. The loop functions when the translation is visible, measurable, and electorally consequential.

Kenya's loop is broken at all three points simultaneously. The translation is invisible — voting records are inaccessible. The gap is unmeasured — no system quantifies the misalignment between MP voting behaviour and constituent preference. The consequence is absent — electoral outcomes in Kenya are determined by ethnicity and patronage, not policy performance, because the policy performance data is never made available in a form that changes votes.

This is not a failure of will. It is a failure of infrastructure. Chanuka is the attempt to build it.

## WHAT THE PLATFORM IS DESIGNED TO DO

Chanuka does six things. Each addresses a specific failure point in the democratic feedback loop. The platform is built but not yet live. These are design intentions whose effectiveness will be tested by the Nairobi pilot.

**1. Extracts and publishes verified voting records.**
The scraping pipeline ingests parliamentary data from parliament.go.ke and the National Assembly Hansard repository, parses per-MP votes from Hansard PDF division tables, normalises MP names across document variants, and assigns a confidence level to every extracted record. Only records meeting the confidence threshold appear on public-facing outputs. A wrong vote attribution on an accountability scorecard is not a minor data error — it is a potentially defamatory and politically weaponisable mistake. The confidence system exists because credibility, once lost, cannot be recovered.

**2. Translates legislation into plain language.**
Bills are summarised in plain language at constituency level — for the citizen in Kibra who was constitutionally guaranteed the right to participate in public participation on the bill but who cannot parse legislative drafting language. This is Article 118 made operational.

**3. Flags constitutional compliance concerns in legislation.**
The platform matches bill provisions against specific constitutional articles, flagging provisions that raise compliance concerns. This is not legal advice and is not presented as such. It is early-warning infrastructure — flags that alert citizens, civil society, and constitutional lawyers to provisions worth examining before a bill completes its parliamentary journey. The methodology is currently being reviewed in partnership with constitutional expertise (Katiba Institute approach in progress).

**4. Calculates the representative gap.**
For each MP in the pilot set, the platform calculates the gap between their voting record and the expressed sentiment of their constituency. This score is an alignment instrument, not a performance metric. An MP with 90% attendance who consistently votes against constituency sentiment scores poorly. An MP with 60% attendance who consistently votes in alignment scores well. The distinction between activity and alignment is the difference between measuring democratic form and measuring democratic function.

**5. Generates auditable participation records.**
The platform creates timestamped, traceable records of public participation processes. For the Parliamentary Service Commission, this means that participation processes facilitated through the platform generate records that can demonstrate constitutional compliance. For civil society organisations challenging legislation, it means that participation records carry evidentiary weight. This addresses directly the procedural failures that led to the Finance Act 2023 and Privatisation Act 2023 nullifications.

**6. Provides universal access.**
The USSD interface for feature phones is the founding design constraint. It was decided before any technical architecture because it is the most important design question the platform answers: *who is allowed into this building, and on what terms?*

## THE THREE AUDIENCES — WHAT EACH HEARS

### Civil society, donors, fellowship programmes: democratic infrastructure

The argument is philosophical and political. Democracy is failing because its infrastructure was never completed. The accountability gap is the gap between constitutional promise and operational reality. Chanuka closes that gap through infrastructure. The USSD decision is a values statement. The confidence-level system is an ethical commitment. The representative gap score is the mechanism by which democratic function is restored.

This framing leads with the global democratic inflection point — the retreat of established democracies from accountability norms, the emboldening of already-compromised governments, the distinction between democratic form and democratic function.

*What this audience funds:* Multi-year grants to build and maintain democratic infrastructure as a public good. No grants have been secured. Applications are in preparation.

### Parliament, constitutional lawyers, legal institutions: compliance infrastructure

The argument is institutional and risk-focused. Parliamentary Clerks face personal liability for procedural failures. Courts have nullified legislation for failure to meet constitutional public participation requirements. The Auditor General is increasing scrutiny. The platform generates auditable participation records and constitutional compliance flags that the Clerk's current systems cannot produce.

This framing leads with institutional risk, not civic mission. The civic mission is present — it is the reason the platform exists — but it is not the lead for an audience whose primary concern is legal exposure.

*What this audience might procure:* Enterprise compliance licence (government procurement, 12–24 month cycle, uncertain outcome). Not secured. A realistic long-term target, not an imminent prospect.

### Civil society organisations and research institutions: participation infrastructure

The argument is operational. CSOs running advocacy campaigns need distribution infrastructure and data to understand where pressure is most likely to be effective. Research institutions and journalists need verified, citable legislative data. The platform is designed to provide both — once it has real users and real data.

*What this audience might purchase:* Partnership access fees once the platform is live and has demonstrated real user engagement. Pre-revenue at present.

## THE REVENUE MODEL — HONEST ACCOUNTING

The platform is civic infrastructure. Civic infrastructure is a public good. Public goods require public funding.

**Primary funding (target 70–80%): Donor grants.**
Multi-year grants from democracy and governance funders — Code for Africa, Mozilla Technology Fund, Ford Foundation, Omidyar/Luminate, Open Society Foundations. None secured at time of writing. These organisations fund public goods that markets will not provide. Chanuka is exactly that.

**High-value but uncertain: Parliament procurement.**
The Parliamentary Service Commission has genuine institutional reasons to procure compliance infrastructure. If secured, a licence value of KSh 50–100M annually would substantially fund operations. Government procurement is a 12–24 month process with political risk. This is worth pursuing with realistic timelines and no assumptions.

**Supplementary: CSO partnerships and research access.**
Achievable once the pilot has demonstrated real user engagement. Not a current revenue source.

**What this model has permanently abandoned:**
Banks. The analysis of the Kenyan banking sector's relationship to accountability infrastructure is unambiguous — banks profit from the opacity the platform reduces, they coast on government bond proceeds, and they have no institutional incentive to invest in democratic accountability. The energy previously spent on a banking sector pitch belongs on the parliamentary procurement track and the donor grant pipeline.

**The right sustainability metric** is not percentage self-funded. It is donor diversification — no single funder exceeding 30% of operating budget.

## WHAT IS TRUE ACROSS ALL THREE LENSES

Six arguments hold regardless of which audience is in the room:

**The accountability gap is real and currently being enforced by courts.** The Finance Act 2023 and Privatisation Act 2023 nullifications are documented. Afrobarometer's 28% parliamentary trust figure is published. The Finance Bill 2024 protests are on record. These are facts independent of any claim Chanuka makes about itself.

**Current solutions are inadequate.** Mzalendo provides transparency, not gap scoring or compliance infrastructure. Law firms provide reactive advisory, not early-warning detection. Parliament's analog systems cannot generate the legally admissible participation records courts now require. The gap in the solution landscape is real.

**The platform creates genuine value, not redistribution.** The citizen who can find their MP's voting record in thirty minutes instead of eight hours has gained something real. The parliamentary clerk who can demonstrate procedural compliance in court has avoided something real. The value is additive and broadly distributed.

**The technical foundation exists.** The platform is built. The scraping pipeline, the constitutional detection methodology, the confidence-level system, the representative gap calculation, the USSD interface — all exist in code. What does not exist yet is a single real user or a single verified live data run. The pilot produces that.

**The constitutional moment is not manufactured urgency.** The 2023–2025 period of legislative volatility and judicial nullification is observable. The courts are enforcing constitutional standards that institutions have failed to operationalise. The urgency in the Chanuka pitch is grounded in events that happened.

**The network effects logic is sound but unproven.** Legislative data accumulates. Constitutional flag methodology improves with each bill analysed. Judicial acceptance of participation records, once established, creates a standard. These are the structural properties of a platform that becomes more valuable over time — but they are structural properties of a platform that does not yet have users. The logic is valid. The evidence does not yet exist.

## WHAT CHANUKA IS NOT

**Not a civic information portal.** Information portals make data available. Chanuka makes data consequential.

**Not a better version of Mzalendo.** Mzalendo measures what MPs do. Chanuka measures the gap between what MPs do and what their constituencies want. These are complementary instruments. The partnership with Mzalendo is a strategic priority.

**Not a technology company.** The compliance infrastructure layer is real and the parliamentary procurement opportunity is real. But the platform's primary identity is democratic infrastructure — a public good designed to be sustained by public funding.

**Not donor-dependent in the pejorative sense.** Public goods are appropriately funded publicly. The risk to manage is donor concentration, not donor funding as a category.

**Not proven.** The platform is built. It has not been tested with real users, real data, or real electoral stakes. The pilot is the first test. Intellectual honesty about this distinction is not weakness — it is the thing that makes every other claim credible.

## THE SINGLE COHERENT ARGUMENT

For any room, any audience, any context:

Kenya's Constitution 2010 promises democratic accountability and lacks the infrastructure to deliver it. That gap is not abstract — it is nullifying legislation in the courts, it is exposing parliamentary clerks to personal liability, and it produced the citizen frustration that drove the Finance Bill 2024 protests. No existing solution closes the full chain from information to participation to evidence to electoral consequence.

Chanuka is the infrastructure designed to close it — built from first principles for the conditions Kenya's democracy actually faces, pre-launch but production-ready in design, intended to be funded as the public good it is, and designed from its first line of code to serve every Kenyan citizen regardless of whether they own a smartphone.

The Nairobi pilot is the first test. East Africa is the scale case. The moment demands completion, not defence.
