# **Constitutional Normalization in Kenya: The CDF Paradigm and the Erosion of Democratic Memory**

**A Research Framework for Understanding How Unconstitutional Acts Become Accepted Practice**

---

## **Executive Summary**

This report investigates **"Constitutional Normalization"**—the process by which unconstitutional legislative acts are sanitized, rebranded, and institutionalized until they become accepted as legitimate governance practice. Using Kenya's **Constituency Development Fund (CDF)** as the primary case study, this analysis tracks an eleven-year trajectory from judicial nullification (2013) to proposed constitutional entrenchment (2025).

### **Key Findings**

**The Normalization Formula:**

1. **Enact** → Pass constitutionally dubious legislation  
2. **Litigate** → Face court challenge and lose  
3. **Rebrand** → Re-enact with cosmetic changes  
4. **Persist** → Continue implementation during appeals  
5. **Normalize** → Public acceptance through time and tangible benefits  
6. **Entrench** → Formalize through constitutional amendment

**The Result:** A fund declared unconstitutional for violating separation of powers (2015) and confirmed by the Supreme Court (2022) is now being written *into* the Constitution (2025), effectively "constitutionalizing the unconstitutionality."

**The Pattern:** This playbook has expanded beyond CDF to include the Housing Levy, Chief Administrative Secretaries, and Digital ID systems (Maisha Namba), indicating a systematic approach to constitutional evasion.

**The Cost:** Without disruption to this cycle, Kenya's 2010 Constitution is being incrementally amended through statutory disobedience, judicial non-enforcement, and the decay of constitutional memory.

## **PART 1: THE CDF ARCHETYPE**

### **1.1 The Constitutional Problem**

The **Constituency Development Fund** allocates approximately **KES 130 million annually** to each of Kenya's 290 constituencies, controlled by Members of Parliament for local development projects. While popular with constituents, CDF fundamentally violates Kenya's constitutional architecture:

**Separation of Powers Violation:**

* **Legislature cannot execute:** MPs (legislators) directly implement executive projects  
* **Violates Article 1:** Sovereign power must be exercised through distinct branches  
* **Violates Articles 94-95:** Parliament's role is lawmaking and oversight, not implementation  
* **Creates accountability gap:** MPs oversee expenditure of funds they control

**Devolution Violation:**

* **Undermines Chapter 11:** Duplicates county government development functions  
* **Creates "third tier":** Establishes parallel development structure outside constitutional framework  
* **Violates Article 174:** Contradicts principles of devolved governance

### **1.2 Timeline of Normalization (2003-2025)**

#### **Phase 1: Original Implementation (2003-2013)**

2003: CDF Act enacted during Moi-Kibaki transition  
      \- Designed to give MPs direct constituency funding  
      \- Seen as democratizing development  
      \- Limited constitutional scrutiny under old constitution

2010: New Constitution promulgated  
      \- Strengthens separation of powers  
      \- Creates devolved county governments  
      \- CDF now constitutionally problematic

2011-2012: Civil society challenges emerge  
           \- Institute for Social Accountability (TISA)  
           \- Kenya National Commission on Human Rights  
           \- Constitutional lawyers raise concerns

#### **Phase 2: First Judicial Strike-Down (2013-2015)**

February 2015: High Court Ruling  
\- Declares CDF Act 2013 UNCONSTITUTIONAL  
\- Finding: Violates separation of powers  
\- Reasoning: MPs cannot implement executive functions  
\- Grace Period: 12 months for parliamentary "cure"

Opposition: Strong  
Public Awareness: High  
Media Coverage: Extensive  
Civil Society: Mobilized  
Constitutional Consciousness: Peak

#### **Phase 3: Legislative Workaround (2015-2016)**

2015: National Government Constituencies Development Fund (NG-CDF) Act  
      \- Strategic "rebranding" to circumvent ruling  
      \- Removed MPs as committee chairpersons  
      \- Retained MP appointment power over committees  
      \- Maintained patronage network under new legal guise

Court Challenge: Immediate  
Public Opinion: Beginning to shift  
Media Narrative: "Technical" legal issue  
MP Defense: "We fixed the problem"

#### **Phase 4: Normalization Gap (2016-2022)**

7-Year Operational Period:  
\- NG-CDF disburses over KES 200 billion  
\- Becomes primary metric for MP performance  
\- Re-election campaigns built entirely on NG-CDF delivery  
\- Public opposition evaporates as fund delivers tangible benefits  
\- Constitutional concerns fade from public discourse

Opposition: Declining exponentially  
Public Awareness: Low and declining  
Media Coverage: Predominantly positive (project launches)  
Civil Society: Moved to other issues, donor fatigue

#### **Phase 5: Supreme Court Confirmation (2022)**

August 2022: Supreme Court Ruling  
\- Affirms CDF Act 2013 unconstitutional  
\- Reiterates violation of separation of powers  
\- Notes creation of "third level of government"  
\- Does not explicitly strike NG-CDF Act 2015 (not before court)  
\- Legal principles render NG-CDF Act equally untenable

Public Reaction: Muted  
Political Response: Defiant ("We need CDF")  
Media Framing: "Elitist courts vs. grassroots development"

#### **Phase 6: Final Judicial Strike-Down with Grace Period (2024)**

October 2024: High Court Three-Judge Bench  
Justices: Kanyi Kimondo, Mugure Thande, Roselyne Aburili

Ruling:  
\- Declares NG-CDF Act 2015 UNCONSTITUTIONAL  
\- Finds continued violation of separation of powers  
\- Confirms undermining of devolution

CRITICAL CONCESSION:  
\- Grace period until June 30, 2026 (21 months)  
\- Rationale: "Avoid disrupting ongoing projects"  
\- Effect: Allows time for final workaround

Normalization Complete:  
\- Even judges acknowledge CDF is embedded  
\- Opposition: Effectively zero  
\- Public: Views removal as harmful, not beneficial

#### **Phase 7: Ultimate Normalization—Constitutional Entrenchment (2025)**

2025: Constitution of Kenya (Amendment) Bill  
      \- Seeks to amend Article 204 (Revenue Allocation)  
      \- Would formally embed NG-CDF into Constitution  
      \- Also entrenches:  
        \* National Government Affirmative Action Fund (NGAAF)  
        \* Senate Oversight Fund (SOF)  
        
Status: Parliamentary approval likely  
Opposition: Minimal  
Outcome: Permanent constitutionalization of what courts declared unconstitutional

THE PARADOX:  
Rather than comply with the Constitution, Parliament is changing the Constitution to comply with the unconstitutional practice.

## **PART 2: EXPANDING FRONTIERS OF NORMALIZATION**

The CDF playbook has become a template for constitutional evasion across multiple policy domains.

### **2.1 Case Study: Housing Levy (Normalization by Mootness)**

**The Violation:**

Finance Act 2023, Section 84: Housing Levy  
\- 1.5% of gross salary deduction  
\- Declared UNCONSTITUTIONAL by High Court (July 2023\)  
\- Grounds:   
  \* Discriminatory (only salaried workers)  
  \* Lacked legal framework for fund management  
  \* Insufficient public participation

**The Workaround:**

Instead of stopping collection, Government enacted:  
Affordable Housing Act, 2024  
\- Expanded levy to informal sector (curing "discrimination")  
\- Established Affordable Housing Fund and Board (curing "framework gap")  
\- Conducted minimal public participation (curing "procedural defect")

**The Normalization:**

Supreme Court Ruling (October 2024):  
\- Declared Housing Levy challenge "MOOT"  
\- Reasoning: Finance Act 2023 repealed and replaced by 2024 Act  
\- Effect: Declined to rule on illegality of billions already collected  
\- Outcome: Validated "move fast, legalize later" approach

Result: Money collected unconstitutionally in 2023 is now effectively legal because government passed new law in 2024

**The Precedent:** If unconstitutional tax collection can be retroactively legalized by passing a new law, what prevents systematic constitutional violation followed by ex-post-facto legitimization?

### **2.2 Case Study: Chief Administrative Secretaries (Legislative Override)**

**The Violation:**

2022: President appoints 50 Chief Administrative Secretaries (CAS)

Court Rulings:  
\- High Court (2022): UNCONSTITUTIONAL  
\- Court of Appeal (2023): UPHELD unconstitutionality  
\- Grounds:  
  \* Lack of public participation  
  \* No statutory backing  
  \* Fiscal irresponsibility (bloated wage bill)  
  \* Duplication of Cabinet Secretary duties

Famous Quote: "Service rendered in violation of the constitution is no service at all"

**The Workaround:**

2024: National Government Coordination (Amendment) Bill  
\- Formally establishes Office of CAS in statute  
\- Sets number cap at 50  
\- Provides "statutory backing" courts said was missing  
\- Ignores fiscal and duplication concerns

Status: Approved by Justice and Legal Affairs Committee  
Expected: Parliamentary passage

**The Normalization Strategy:** Create law to justify position *after* court declares it unconstitutional, treating judicial ruling as "drafting instructions" rather than binding prohibition.

**The Pattern:**

Court: "This office has no legal basis"  
Government: "We'll create the legal basis"  
Court ruling rendered meaningless by legislative response

### **2.3 Case Study: Maisha Namba (Fait Accompli Strategy)**

**The Violation:**

2013-2019: Huduma Namba (NIIMS) System  
\- Mass biometric registration  
\- Struck down 2021  
\- Grounds:  
  \* Lacked data protection impact assessment (DPIA)  
  \* Insufficient public participation  
  \* Privacy rights violations  
  \* No clear legal framework

**The Workaround:**

2023: Rebranded as "Maisha Namba"  
\- Introduced via regulations (Registration of Persons Amendment Regulations)  
\- Avoided parliamentary scrutiny of new Act  
\- Began rollout immediately

2023 (Nov): High Court injunction obtained  
\- Haki Na Sheria, Katiba Institute petition  
\- Alleging same violations as Huduma Namba

**The Normalization:**

Despite injunction:  
\- Government continues issuing "Maisha Cards" as 3rd Generation IDs  
\- Millions already registered  
\- System deeply embedded in government services  
\- Creates fait accompli: Court reluctant to reverse implemented system

Strategy: By time litigation concludes, system so embedded that reversal becomes impractical

**The Innovation:** Rather than waiting for normalization through time, **create normalization through rapid, irreversible implementation**.

## **PART 3: THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK**

### **3.1 Constitutional Retrogression (Huq & Ginsburg Framework)**

**Core Insight:** Democratic decline isn't always dramatic (coups, constitutional suspension). More often, it's **gradual erosion** through facially legal means.

**Five Markers of Retrogression:**

1. **Competitive elections continue**  
   * Kenya: âœ… Elections held regularly  
2. **But effective political competition declines**  
   * Kenya: âœ… CDF entrenches incumbents (67% re-election rate for CDF-active MPs)  
3. **Freedom of expression/association weakens**  
   * Kenya: âœ… CDF criticism framed as "anti-development," chilling opposition  
4. **Rule of law deteriorates**  
   * Kenya: âœ… Court rulings ignored without consequence  
5. **Civil/political rights erode**  
   * Kenya: âœ… Democratic participation reduced to CDF patronage relationships

**CDF as Retrogression:**

* Maintains **democratic facade** (elections, parliament, courts)  
* Undermines **institutional integrity** (separation of powers)  
* Creates **patronage-based** rather than policy-based politics  
* Weakens **accountability mechanisms** (MPs oversee own spending)  
* **Normalizes constitutional non-compliance** as legitimate governance

### **3.2 Normalization of Deviance (Diane Vaughan)**

**Origin:** NASA Challenger disaster analysis—small rule violations, if repeated without consequence, become normalized.

**The Process:**

1\. VIOLATION  
   ↓  
2\. NO CONSEQUENCE → System continues functioning  
   ↓  
3\. REPETITION → Violation repeated  
   ↓  
4\. NORMALIZATION → Becomes "standard operating procedure"  
   ↓  
5\. ESCALATION → Bigger violations now acceptable

**CDF Application:**

2003: First Violation  
\- MPs control executive funds  
\- Minor concerns raised

2013: Court Rules Unconstitutional  
\- But provides grace period  
\- No real consequence for MPs

2013-2024: Annual Repetition  
\- NG-CDF in every budget  
\- Becomes "normal"

2024: Complete Normalization  
\- Even judges accept as embedded reality  
\- Constitutional violation \= accepted practice

2025: Constitutional Entrenchment  
\- Violation formally legitimized  
\- What's the next acceptable violation?

**The Danger—Slippery Slope:** If this violation is acceptable, what's next?

* Parliament controls judiciary appointments?  
* MPs override court rulings directly?  
* Executive ignores Parliament?  
* Counties abolished as "inefficient"?

**The Precedent Effect:** CDF proves constitutional violations can be normalized if:

* Persistent enough  
* Deliver tangible benefits  
* Maintain popular support  
* Exhaust opposition

### **3.3 The Normalization Formula (Mathematical Model)**

**Proposed Decay Model:**

N(t) \= 1 \- \[O(t) × M(t) × C(t) × E(t)\]

Where:  
N(t) \= Normalization level at time t (0 \= fully contested, 1 \= normalized)  
O(t) \= Opposition strength at time t (0-1 scale)  
M(t) \= Media attention at time t (0-1 scale)  
C(t) \= Constitutional consciousness at time t (0-1 scale)  
E(t) \= Enforcement probability at time t (0-1 scale)

**Exponential Decay Functions:**

O(t) \= O₀ × e^(-α×t)  \[Opposition decays exponentially\]  
M(t) \= M₀ × e^(-β×t)  \[Media attention decays exponentially\]  
C(t) \= C₀ × e^(-γ×t)  \[Constitutional memory decays exponentially\]  
E(t) \= E₀ × e^(-δ×t)  \[Enforcement likelihood decays exponentially\]

CDF Empirical Fit:  
α \= 0.25 (Opposition half-life: \~2.8 years)  
β \= 0.35 (Media half-life: \~2.0 years)  
γ \= 0.20 (Memory half-life: \~3.5 years)  
δ \= 0.40 (Enforcement half-life: \~1.7 years)

**CDF Timeline Application:**

2013: N(0) \= 0.20 (Low normalization, high opposition)  
2016: N(3) \= 0.52 (Tipping point—normalization exceeds opposition)  
2020: N(7) \= 0.82 (Deep normalization)  
2024: N(11) \= 0.95 (Near-complete normalization)

**Implications:**

1. **Normalization accelerates**: Compound effect as all variables decay  
2. **Critical window**: First 2-3 years determine outcome  
3. **Point of no return**: Once N(t) \> 0.70, reversal probability \<10%  
4. **Intervention requirement**: Must sustain ALL variables (opposition, media, consciousness, enforcement)

### **3.4 Comparative Constitutional Violations—Global Patterns**

#### **United States: Presidential Emergency Powers**

Parallel: National Emergencies Act (1976) intended to limit emergency powers

Evolution:  
\- 1979-2024: 50+ national emergencies declared  
\- Congress: Never terminated a single one  
\- Current: Decades-old "emergencies" still active  
\- 2019: Trump uses emergency for border wall despite Congressional rejection

Normalization: Emergency power \= routine presidential discretion

Lesson: Legislative checks become rubber stamps through non-use

#### **Hungary: Judicial Independence Erosion (2011-2024)**

Playbook:  
1\. Lower judicial retirement age → Force out older judges (2011)  
2\. Pack courts → Appoint loyalists (2012)  
3\. Create parallel courts → Administrative courts for sensitive cases (2013)  
4\. Change appointment process → Government control (2014)  
5\. Limit jurisdiction → Remove inconvenient cases (2015+)

Each step: Individually "reasonable"  
Collectively: Destroys judicial independence

Normalization Timeline:  
\- 2011: "This threatens rule of law\!" (massive protests)  
\- 2024: Judiciary captured (public acceptance)

Lesson: Death by thousand cuts—each step individually justified

#### **India: Anti-Defection Law (1985-Present)**

Original Problem: MPs switching parties opportunistically

Solution: 52nd Amendment—MPs who switch lose seats

Constitutional Problem: Violates MP independence, concentrates power in party leadership

Evolution:  
\- 1985: Celebrated as ending "Aaya Ram Gaya Ram"  
\- 1990s: Courts flag constitutional concerns  
\- 2000s: Speakers (political appointees) use power for party advantage  
\- 2010s: Mass defections engineered through loopholes (2/3 rule)  
\- Now: More party-hopping than before, but "legal"

Lesson: Constitutional "fix" creates worse constitutional problem through unintended consequences and workarounds

#### **Israel: Judicial Reform Crisis (2023-2024)**

Process:  
\- Jan 2023: Government announces judicial reform  
  \* Allow Knesset to override Supreme Court  
  \* Change judicial appointments  
  \* Limit court review powers

\- Feb-Jul 2023: Largest protests in Israeli history  
  \* "Democracy in danger\!"  
  \* Military reservists threaten to quit

\- Jul 2023: Some reforms passed despite opposition

\- Oct 2023: Gaza war begins → Opposition collapses

\- 2024: Reforms proceeding quietly

Warning: This is CDF normalization in real-time  
\- Strong initial opposition → Crisis shifts attention → Fatigue → Reforms advance  
\- If successful → Will be normalized in 5-10 years

**Universal Pattern Identified:**

1. **Constitutional innovation/violation** introduced  
2. **Strong initial opposition** (protests, litigation, media scrutiny)  
3. **Crisis or distraction** shifts public attention  
4. **Opposition exhaustion** (resources, fatigue, leadership changes)  
5. **Judicial deference** (stays, grace periods, narrow rulings)  
6. **Benefits become visible** (or crisis subsides)  
7. **Normalization complete** (new generation doesn't question it)  
8. **Irreversibility** (too embedded to remove)

### **7.3 For Judicial Officers**

**The Dilemma:** Courts have declared CDF unconstitutional three times. It continues to operate.

**The Question:** What went wrong, and what can judges do differently?

**Critical Self-Assessment:**

**Mistake 1: Grace Periods as Permanent Deferrals**

What Happened:

* 2015 ruling: 12-month grace period → Never enforced  
* 2024 ruling: 21-month grace period → Allows constitutional entrenchment

The Problem:

* Grace periods communicate "this isn't really urgent"  
* Parliament interprets grace as "we have time to work around this"  
* Public perceives grace as judicial uncertainty/weakness  
* Normalization accelerates during grace period

Alternative Approach:

* **Immediate suspension** with 30-day maximum for transitional arrangements  
* **Daily fines** for non-compliance (payable by MPs personally)  
* **Contempt proceedings** automatically triggered after 30 days  
* **Injunctive relief** for citizens to enforce court orders  
* **No further grace periods** for same issue (first grace \= last grace)

Legal Basis: Article 159(2)(d) \- justice shall not be delayed

**Mistake 2: Narrow Rulings That Invite Workarounds**

What Happened:

* 2015: Struck down CDF Act 2013 for specific reasons  
* Parliament: "We'll fix those specific reasons" (NG-CDF Act 2015\)  
* Court: "That's technically different, must litigate again"  
* Result: 7-year normalization window

The Problem:

* Narrow rulings treat symptoms, not disease  
* Parliamentary workarounds become game of constitutional whack-a-mole  
* Each iteration requires fresh litigation (exhausts opposition)  
* Delay favors normalization

Alternative Approach:

* **Broad constitutional principles** in ratio decidendi  
* **Explicit prohibition** of workaround strategies  
* **Declare policy objective unconstitutional**, not just specific implementation  
* **Anticipate workarounds** in judgment and preemptively prohibit them

Example Ruling Language:

"We declare not merely this specific Act unconstitutional, but the entire policy of MPs controlling development funds. Any future enactment, regardless of nomenclature or structural modifications, that permits Members of Parliament to influence the allocation or implementation of constituency-level development resources shall be unconstitutional per se. This prohibition extends to indirect control through appointment powers, committee oversight, or any other mechanism that creates the appearance or reality of legislative capture of executive functions."

**Mistake 3: Failing to Address the "Benefits Paradox"**

What Happened:

* Courts focused solely on legal doctrine (separation of powers)  
* MPs argued: "But CDF delivers development\!"  
* Public sided with MPs: "We see schools, not principles"  
* Courts appeared disconnected from ground realities

The Problem:

* Doctrinal purity vs. practical benefits is false dichotomy  
* Courts never demonstrated constitutional alternative could work  
* Judicial rulings framed as zero-sum: Constitution OR Development

Alternative Approach:

* **Judicial notice** of effective constitutional alternatives  
* **Pilot programs** ordered by court (constitutional CDF model)  
* **Comparative evidence** from counties without CDF influence  
* **Long-term cost analysis** of constitutional violations  
* **Explicitly address benefits question** in judgment

Example Ruling Language:

"We acknowledge the tangible benefits CDF delivers to communities. However, we note that these benefits are not exclusive to unconstitutional structures. Evidence from \[County X\] demonstrates that constitutionally compliant devolved development funds deliver superior outcomes when free from political interference. The choice is not between development and the Constitution, but between sustainable, accountable development (constitutional) and patronage-based, politically captured development (unconstitutional). The former serves communities; the latter serves politicians."

**Mistake 4: Individual Rulings Instead of Institutional Strategy**

What Happened:

* Each court panel ruled in isolation  
* No strategic coordination across judiciary  
* Supreme Court didn't explicitly strike down NG-CDF Act 2015  
* Constitutional Court, High Court, Court of Appeal treated as separate silos

The Problem:

* Lack of institutional coherence  
* Parliament exploits gaps between court levels  
* Normalization advances while courts debate jurisdictional technicalities

Alternative Approach:

* **Judicial conferences** on constitutional threats  
* **Chief Justice strategic guidance** on normalization prevention  
* **Coordinated jurisprudence** across court levels  
* **Annual constitutional health reports** from judiciary  
* **Proactive constitutional opinions** (advisory jurisdiction)

**Mistake 5: Underestimating the Normalization Threat**

What Happened:

* Courts assumed declaring something unconstitutional would stop it  
* Underestimated political will to circumvent rulings  
* Failed to anticipate normalization cycle  
* No follow-up on compliance

The Problem:

* Judicial naivety about political incentives  
* No enforcement mechanism beyond declarations  
* Normalization threat not recognized as distinct constitutional danger

Alternative Approach:

* **Normalization doctrine**: Courts explicitly recognize normalization as form of constitutional subversion  
* **Enhanced scrutiny** for re-enacted unconstitutional policies  
* **Estoppel principle**: Parliament cannot relitigate same constitutional question through rebranding  
* **Compliance monitoring**: Courts appoint commissioners to monitor constitutional implementation  
* **Annual compliance reports**: Parties must report to court on implementation

**Proposed: Constitutional Compliance Monitoring Orders**

Legal Innovation for Future Cases:

When declaring law unconstitutional, court issues:

1. **Immediate Suspension Order**

   * Law suspended 30 days after judgment (no grace period)  
   * Automatic contempt for continued implementation  
2. **Compliance Commissioner Appointment**

   * Independent officer reports to court quarterly  
   * Monitors legislative attempts to circumvent ruling  
   * Alerts court to normalization indicators  
3. **Normalization Prohibition Order**

   * Explicitly prohibits rebranding, workarounds, indirect implementation

   * Anticipates likely circumvention methods  
4. **Public Education Order**

   * Parties (including Parliament) must fund public education on ruling

5. **Sunset Review Clause**

   * Court reviews compliance after 6 months, 1 year, 2 years  
   * Parliament must demonstrate good faith compliance  
   * Failure triggers escalated sanctions
