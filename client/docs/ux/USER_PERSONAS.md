# Chanuka User Personas

## Overview

This document defines the primary user personas for the Chanuka Legislative Transparency Platform. These personas are grounded in the reality that **legislative transparency alone doesn't create accountability**—it requires organized intermediaries who can translate information into political pressure.

### Critical Assumptions

**Who Actually Changes Legislation?**
- Individual citizens rarely influence policy directly
- Organized groups with sustained engagement drive change
- Media amplification creates political cost for MPs
- Economic stakeholders have disproportionate influence
- International pressure matters for certain bill types

**The Information-to-Action Gap**
Most citizens lack:
- Time to engage deeply with legislation
- Legal literacy to understand implications
- Social capital to influence representatives
- Economic security to prioritize civic engagement

**Our Strategic Bet**
We optimize for **force multipliers**—users who can:
1. Translate complex legislation for mass audiences
2. Organize collective action at scale
3. Create political consequences for MPs
4. Sustain pressure across legislative cycles

This means prioritizing journalists, advocates, and organized groups over individual casual users, while maintaining accessibility for all.

## Persona 1: Organized Citizen Group Leader (Wanjiku)

### Profile
- **Name**: Wanjiku Mwangi
- **Age**: 32
- **Occupation**: Small business owner (M-Pesa agent) + Informal sector association treasurer
- **Location**: Gikomba Market, Nairobi
- **Tech Proficiency**: Moderate (smartphone primary device)
- **Civic Engagement**: High within her network (500+ traders)
- **Political Capital**: Moderate (constituency-level connections)

### Critical Context
**Why Wanjiku Matters More Than Random Citizens:**
- Represents 500+ informal traders who vote as a bloc
- Her WhatsApp group reaches 3 constituencies
- Local MP attends their association meetings
- Can mobilize physical protests (50-100 people)
- Trusted voice in community (10+ years in market)

**Her Real Constraints:**
- 2-3 hours per week max for civic work (not daily)
- Needs information she can delegate to others
- Must justify time spent to family/business
- Requires tangible wins to maintain group engagement
- Limited English literacy (Swahili primary)

### Demographics
- **Device**: Android smartphone (mid-range, 2-3 years old)
- **Connection**: 3G/4G mobile data (500MB weekly bundle)
- **Language**: Swahili primary, basic English
- **Usage Time**: 20-30 minutes per session, 2-3x per week
- **Network Size**: 500+ direct, 2000+ indirect reach

### Goals
**Primary (What She'll Actually Use Platform For):**
- Find bills affecting informal sector BEFORE they pass
- Get Swahili summaries she can share in WhatsApp groups
- Track which MPs support/oppose trader interests
- Coordinate with other market associations
- Build evidence for meetings with MP

**Secondary (Aspirational):**
- Understand county vs national legislation
- Monitor implementation of passed bills
- Connect with other advocacy groups

### Pain Points
**Technical:**
- Limited data bundle (needs aggressive caching)
- Unreliable connection in market area
- Shares phone with family members
- Complex legal language is barrier

**Structural:**
- Bills move too fast to organize response
- Doesn't know which bills matter until too late
- Hard to coordinate across multiple markets
- MPs ignore individual complaints
- No feedback loop on advocacy efforts

**Social:**
- Seen as "troublemaker" by some traders
- Time away from business costs money
- Family questions civic engagement value
- Burnout from past failed campaigns

### Needs
**Critical (Must-Have):**
- **Swahili-First**: All summaries in Swahili, English secondary
- **WhatsApp Integration**: Share-ready formats for groups
- **Offline-First**: Download weekly digest, read offline
- **Alert Filtering**: Only bills affecting informal sector
- **MP Accountability**: Voting records on trader issues

**Important (Should-Have):**
- **Coalition Building**: Connect with similar groups
- **Template Actions**: Pre-written petition/letter templates
- **Impact Tracking**: Show when advocacy worked
- **Audio Summaries**: For low-literacy members

**Nice-to-Have:**
- **SMS Alerts**: For critical bills (data-free)
- **Meeting Scheduler**: Coordinate group actions
- **Media Kit**: Press release templates

### User Journey (Realistic)
**Weekly Routine:**
1. Sunday evening: Opens app on WiFi at home
2. Downloads weekly digest (5-10 bills flagged)
3. Reads Swahili summaries (10 minutes)
4. Identifies 1-2 bills affecting traders
5. Shares to 3 WhatsApp groups with her commentary
6. Monitors group discussion (passive)

**When Bill is Critical:**
1. Receives SMS alert (data-free)
2. Opens app, reads detailed summary
3. Calls 5 other market leaders (voice)
4. Schedules physical meeting
5. Uses app to show MP voting record
6. Coordinates petition using template
7. Tracks bill status until vote

**Quarterly:**
1. Reviews MP scorecard before meeting
2. Exports PDF for association records
3. Shares wins with group to maintain momentum

### Design Implications
**Technical:**
- Swahili as default language (not English)
- Aggressive offline caching (weekly digest)
- WhatsApp-optimized sharing (images + text)
- SMS fallback for critical alerts
- Low-bandwidth mode always on
- Progressive Web App (no app store barrier)

**Content:**
- Plain Swahili summaries (not translations)
- Focus on economic impact (not legal theory)
- Show which MPs support traders
- Template actions (copy-paste ready)
- Success stories (proof it works)

**Features:**
- Group coordination tools
- Coalition discovery (find similar groups)
- Impact tracking (show advocacy results)
- Audio summaries (accessibility + literacy)

### Success Metrics
**Engagement:**
- Weekly active usage (not daily)
- WhatsApp shares per bill
- Group coordination events
- Template downloads

**Impact:**
- Bills where traders mobilized
- MP meetings influenced by platform data
- Policy changes attributed to advocacy
- Network growth (new market associations)

### Why This Persona Drives Impact
- **Scale**: Reaches 2000+ voters through networks
- **Credibility**: Trusted community voice (10+ years)
- **Persistence**: Sustained engagement (not one-off)
- **Political Leverage**: MPs care about organized blocs
- **Force Multiplier**: Translates info for low-literacy members

### Demographics
- **Device**: Laptop (newsroom) + smartphone (field)
- **Connection**: Reliable WiFi (office) + 4G (mobile)
- **Language**: English primary, Swahili fluent
- **Usage Time**: 30-60 minutes per story, 2-3x per week
- **Audience Reach**: 2M+ direct, 5M+ indirect

### Goals
**Primary (What Drives Story Selection):**
- Find bills with corruption/conflict of interest angles
- Expose MPs voting against constituent interests
- Track money/power behind legislation
- Break stories before competitors
- Build investigative series (long-term impact)

**Secondary:**
- Verify citizen complaints about bills
- Fact-check MP claims about legislation
- Monitor implementation of passed bills
- Build source network in Parliament

### Pain Points
**Editorial:**
- Editor wants "sexy" stories (not policy wonkery)
- Needs human interest angle for TV
- Complex bills hard to explain in 2 minutes
- Readers glaze over at legal details
- Competition from clickbait

**Practical:**
- Bills buried in parliamentary jargon
- Hard to track amendments/changes
- MPs dodge accountability questions
- Lobbyist influence is hidden
- No time for deep research

**Verification:**
- Must independently verify all claims
- Needs quotable sources (not just data)
- Risk of libel if wrong
- MPs threaten legal action
- Platform data must be bulletproof

### Needs
**Critical (Must-Have):**
- **Corruption Signals**: Flag bills with conflicts of interest
- **Voting Accountability**: Which MPs voted how + why it matters

- **Export Evidence**: Court-ready documentation

**Important (Should-Have):**
- **Lobbyist Tracking**: Who's pushing which bills
- **Financial Impact**: Cost to taxpayers/citizens
- **Comparative Analysis**: Similar bills in other countries
- **Source Protection**: Secure tips/leaks
- **Trend Detection**: Patterns across bills

**Nice-to-Have:**
- **API Access**: Automated monitoring
- **Collaboration**: Share research with other journalists
- **Embargo System**: Coordinate story timing
- **Media Kit**: Ready-made graphics/charts

### User Journey (Realistic)
**Weekly Monitoring:**
1. Monday morning: Checks "newsworthy bills" dashboard
2. Scans for corruption/conflict signals (5 minutes)
3. Identifies 2-3 potential stories
4. Pitches to editor (1 gets approved)

**Story Development (24-48 hours):**
1. Deep dive on approved bill
2. Reviews voting records + MP statements
3. Identifies conflicts of interest
4. Exports timeline + voting data
5. Calls MPs for comment (uses data as leverage)
6. Writes story with platform citations
7. Sets alert for bill updates

**Investigative Series (2-3 months):**
1. Uses API to analyze voting patterns
2. Identifies systematic corruption
3. Builds evidence over multiple bills
4. Coordinates with civil society
5. Times story for maximum impact
6. Triggers parliamentary investigation

### Design Implications
**Technical:**
- "Newsworthy" algorithm (corruption, conflict, impact)
- Verified data only (no speculation)
- Citation-ready exports (PDF, CSV)
- API for automated monitoring
- Secure communication (source protection)

**Content:**
- Lead with "why this matters" (not legal details)
- Show human impact (not just policy)
- Expose hidden interests (follow the money)
- Provide quotable statistics
- Timeline of bill manipulation

**Features:**
- Corruption/conflict detection
- MP accountability scorecards
- Lobbyist influence tracking
- Comparative international analysis
- Secure document submission
- Collaboration tools (multi-reporter)

### Success Metrics
**Engagement:**
- Stories published using platform
- Citations in major media
- Parliamentary questions triggered
- Investigations launched

**Impact:**
- Bills amended due to coverage
- MPs held accountable
- Public awareness increased
- Policy changes attributed to journalism

### Why This Persona Drives Impact
- **Amplification**: Reaches millions instantly
- **Political Cost**: MPs fear negative coverage
- **Permanence**: Creates searchable record
- **Validation**: Legitimizes citizen concerns
- **Cascade Effect**: Other media follow her lead

### Platform's Value Proposition to Sarah
**Time Savings:**
- Finds newsworthy bills in minutes (not hours)
- Pre-verified data (no manual fact-checking)
- Ready-made visualizations (TV-ready)

**Competitive Edge:**
- Break stories before competitors
- Deeper analysis than other outlets
- Exclusive data/insights

**Impact:**
- Stories lead to real policy changes
- Build reputation as accountability journalist
- Win journalism awards

**Risk Reduction:**

- Transparent methodology
- Independent verification

## Persona 5: Accessibility-First User (Njeri)

### Profile
- **Name**: Njeri Muthoni
- **Age**: 38
- **Occupation**: Disability rights advocate + Legal aid paralegal
- **Location**: Mombasa, Kenya
- **Tech Proficiency**: High (assistive tech expert)
- **Civic Engagement**: High (professional + personal)
- **Political Capital**: Moderate (represents 2.2M disabled Kenyans)

### Critical Context
**Why Njeri is Both User and Litmus Test:**
- Represents 2.2 million disabled Kenyans (10% of voters)
- If platform doesn't work for her, it's broken (not niche)
- Accessibility = usability for everyone
- Her needs expose design flaws others tolerate
- Legal mandate (Kenya Persons with Disabilities Act)

**Her Real Power:**
- Can mobilize disability rights movement
- Legal expertise in discrimination cases
- Influences disability-related legislation
- Trains other advocates on accessibility
- Holds government accountable to CRPD

**Her Constraints:**
- Most platforms ignore accessibility (exhausting)
- Assistive tech is expensive (NVDA is free)
- Internet cafes lack accessibility features
- Many disabled Kenyans have low literacy
- Stigma limits political participation

### Demographics
- **Device**: Desktop with NVDA screen reader (office) + smartphone (personal)
- **Connection**: Reliable internet (office), limited mobile data
- **Language**: English primary, Swahili fluent
- **Usage Time**: 1-2 hours per day, 4-5 days per week
- **Assistive Tech**: NVDA screen reader, keyboard-only navigation
- **Network Size**: 2.2M disabled Kenyans, 500+ active advocates

### Goals
**Primary (Professional):**
- Monitor bills affecting disability rights
- Advocate for inclusive legislation
- Provide legal aid on discrimination cases
- Train other disabled advocates
- Hold MPs accountable on disability issues

**Secondary (Personal):**
- Participate fully in civic life
- Prove disabled people can lead advocacy
- Change attitudes about disability
- Build accessible advocacy infrastructure

### Pain Points
**Technical (Universal):**
- Most sites lack proper ARIA labels (screen reader fails)
- Complex interactions break with keyboard
- Images without alt text (information loss)
- Auto-playing content is disruptive
- Poor heading structure (navigation nightmare)
- Focus indicators invisible (lost on page)

**Kenyan Context:**
- Government sites worst offenders
- No accessibility testing in procurement
- Developers don't know WCAG exists
- "We'll add accessibility later" (never happens)
- Disability seen as charity, not rights

**Advocacy:**
- Hard to prove discrimination without data
- MPs ignore disability constituency
- Bills rarely consider disability impact
- Accessibility amendments always cut (cost)
- Disabled advocates excluded from consultations

### Needs
**Critical (Legal Requirement):**
- **WCAG 2.1 AA Minimum**: Legal compliance (Kenya PWD Act)
- **Full Keyboard Access**: All features keyboard-navigable
- **Screen Reader Support**: Proper ARIA labels, semantic HTML
- **Clear Structure**: Logical heading hierarchy (H1-H6)
- **Skip Links**: Jump to main content
- **No Keyboard Traps**: Can always escape
- **Focus Indicators**: Always visible

**Important (Usability):**
- **Plain Language**: Simple explanations (cognitive accessibility)
- **Consistent Navigation**: Predictable patterns
- **Error Prevention**: Clear validation messages
- **Sufficient Contrast**: WCAG AAA preferred
- **Resizable Text**: 200% without breaking
- **No Time Limits**: Or easy to extend

**Nice-to-Have (Enhanced):**
- **Audio Summaries**: For low-literacy users
- **Sign Language Videos**: For deaf community
- **Easy Read Versions**: Cognitive accessibility
- **Customizable Interface**: User preferences
- **Accessibility Statement**: Transparent compliance

### User Journey (Realistic)
**Daily Monitoring:**
1. Opens platform with keyboard (Tab key)
2. Uses skip link to main content (bypass navigation)
3. Screen reader announces new bills (ARIA live region)
4. Navigates by headings (H1-H6 structure)
5. Activates bill with Enter key
6. Listens to summary (screen reader)
7. Saves bill (keyboard shortcut)

**When Disability Bill Appears:**
1. Deep analysis of disability impact
2. Identifies accessibility gaps
3. Drafts amendment language
4. Coordinates with disability organizations
5. Provides testimony to committee
6. Tracks bill progress
7. Mobilizes disabled voters if needed

**Accessibility Testing (Ongoing):**
1. Tests new features with screen reader
2. Reports accessibility bugs
3. Suggests improvements
4. Validates fixes
5. Trains other disabled users

### Design Implications
**Technical (Non-Negotiable):**
- Semantic HTML (not div soup)
- Proper ARIA labels on all interactive elements
- Keyboard navigation for everything
- Focus management (modals, dynamic content)
- Skip links on every page
- No keyboard traps
- Visible focus indicators (3:1 contrast minimum)
- ARIA live regions for dynamic updates
- Form labels and error messages
- Alt text on all images (or role="presentation")

**Content:**
- Plain language (8th grade reading level)
- Consistent terminology
- Clear headings (describe content)
- Descriptive link text (not "click here")
- Error messages explain how to fix

**Testing:**
- Automated testing (axe, WAVE)
- Manual keyboard testing
- Screen reader testing (NVDA, JAWS)
- User testing with disabled people
- Continuous monitoring

### Success Metrics
**Compliance:**
- WCAG 2.1 AA conformance (100%)
- Zero critical accessibility bugs
- Keyboard navigation complete
- Screen reader compatibility

**Usage:**
- Disabled user adoption rate
- Accessibility feature usage
- Bug reports from disabled users
- Advocacy campaigns led by disabled users

**Impact:**
- Disability bills monitored
- Accessibility amendments proposed
- Disabled voter mobilization
- Platform becomes accessibility model

### Why This Persona is Non-Negotiable
**Legal:**
- Kenya Persons with Disabilities Act requires accessibility
- UN CRPD (Kenya is signatory)
- Discrimination lawsuits if non-compliant

**Moral:**
- 2.2M Kenyans have right to participate
- Disability is not niche (10% of population)

**Practical:**

## Design Decision Framework

### When Building Features, Ask:

**1. Force Multiplier Test**
- Does this help journalists break stories?
- Does this enable advocates to organize campaigns?
- Does this empower community leaders?
- If no to all three, reconsider priority

**2. Scale Test**
- Can this user reach 1000+ people?
- Can they sustain pressure over months?
- Can they create political consequences?
- If no to all three, lower priority

**3. Accessibility Test**
- Can Njeri use this independently?
- Does it work with keyboard only?
- Does screen reader announce correctly?
- If no to any, block launch

**4. Impact Test**
- Will this change legislation?
- Will this hold MPs accountable?
- Will this mobilize collective action?
- If no to all three, reconsider

**5. Sustainability Test**
- Can we maintain this feature?
- Does it generate revenue or impact?
- Will users actually use it?
- If no to all three, don't build

### Feature Prioritization Matrix

**Build First (High Impact + High Reach):**
- Journalist tools (newsworthy detection, citations)
- Advocate tools (campaign management, coalitions)
- Community tools (WhatsApp sharing, Swahili)
- Accessibility (keyboard, screen reader, ARIA)

**Build Second (High Impact OR High Reach):**
- Economic stakeholder tools (early warning, analysis)
- Electoral accountability (MP scorecards, voting records)
- Data exports (CSV, PDF, API)
- Collaboration features (shared collections)

**Build Third (Nice-to-Have):**
- Advanced analytics (trend detection)
- International comparisons
- Historical archives
- Academic research tools

**Don't Build (Low Impact + Low Reach):**
- Gamification for casual users
- Social features without organization
- Complex visualizations for general public
- Features that don't drive accountability

## Critical Tensions & Trade-offs

### Tension 1: Accessibility vs. Power User Features
**Conflict:**
- Njeri needs simple, predictable interfaces
- James needs complex, powerful tools
- Can't optimize for both simultaneously

**Resolution:**
- Progressive disclosure (simple by default, power available)
- Keyboard shortcuts don't break screen readers
- Advanced features in separate sections
- Both personas test every feature

**Non-Negotiable:**
- Accessibility always wins conflicts
- Power features must be accessible
- If can't make accessible, don't build

### Tension 2: Transparency vs. Privacy
**Conflict:**
- Platform should expose lobbying (Dr. Kamau)
- Users need private workspaces (campaigns)
- Transparency can endanger advocates

**Resolution:**
- Public data is always public (bills, votes)
- User activity is private by default
- Industry lobbying is disclosed
- Advocates can work privately
- Publish aggregated impact data

**Non-Negotiable:**
- No secret influence on legislation
- Protect advocate safety
- Disclose conflicts of interest

### Tension 3: Revenue vs. Mission
**Conflict:**
- Need revenue to sustain platform
- Can't charge citizens for democracy
- Industry will pay for premium access
- Creates two-tier system

**Resolution:**
- Free tier for all core features
- Premium tier for industry/institutions
- Revenue funds public access
- No exclusive early access
- Transparent pricing

**Non-Negotiable:**
- Core accountability features always free
- No pay-to-win for lobbying
- Revenue doesn't compromise mission

### Tension 4: Scale vs. Quality
**Conflict:**
- Want millions of users (scale)
- Force multipliers drive impact (quality)
- Can't optimize for both

**Resolution:**
- Optimize for force multipliers first
- Enable them to reach masses
- Measure impact, not just users
- Quality users > quantity

**Non-Negotiable:**
- Impact over vanity metrics
- Don't sacrifice quality for scale
- Force multipliers are primary users

### Tension 5: Kenyan Context vs. International Standards
**Conflict:**
- Swahili-first for Kenyan users
- English for international donors/media
- Local context vs. global best practices

**Resolution:**
- Swahili and English equal priority
- Kenyan context drives design
- International standards (WCAG, etc.)
- Local solutions, global quality

**Non-Negotiable:**
- Swahili is not secondary
- Kenyan users are primary
- International standards are minimum

## Uncomfortable Truths We Must Accept

### 1. Power is Unequal
- Dr. Kamau has more influence than 10,000 citizens
- MPs prioritize donors over constituents
- Economic leverage > democratic voice
- Platform can't fix this, only expose it

**Our Response:**
- Make power visible
- Enable counter-lobbying
- Create political cost for capture
- Don't pretend equality exists

### 2. Most Citizens Won't Engage Deeply
- People are busy, tired, struggling
- Civic engagement is luxury for many
- Information alone doesn't create action
- Rational voter theory is myth

**Our Response:**
- Optimize for intermediaries
- Enable organized collective action
- Make engagement low-friction
- Don't blame citizens for system failure

### 3. Ethnicity Often Trumps Policy
- Voters prioritize ethnic solidarity
- MPs rely on ethnic voting blocs
- Policy accountability is secondary
- This is rational given patronage system

**Our Response:**
- Don't ignore this reality
- Show when MPs betray ethnic base
- Highlight cross-ethnic coalitions
- Build alternative accountability

### 4. Transparency Can Endanger Advocates
- Government retaliates against critics
- "Foreign agent" accusations are real
- Security concerns are legitimate
- Visibility can be dangerous

**Our Response:**
- Private workspaces for campaigns
- Secure communication channels
- Protect user identity
- Don't force public participation

### 5. We Will Enable Bad Actors
- Lobbyists will use platform
- Misinformation will spread
- Tools can be weaponized
- Can't prevent all abuse

**Our Response:**
- Make activity visible
- Enable counter-speech
- Moderate clear violations
- Accept imperfect system
